# Topological Business Cycles: Zigzag Persistence and Labour Market Fragmentation in the UK, 1991–2022

**Authors:** Stephen Dorman
**Target Journal:** *Sociological Methods & Research*
**Date:** March 2026 (First Draft)

---

## Abstract

Cross-sectional topological summaries of a point cloud cannot distinguish structural change from stable heterogeneity. We track how the topology of the UK employment-income trajectory space evolves across 32 annual cohort snapshots (1991–2022) by applying zigzag persistent homology — a method that computes a single barcode over a temporal tower of simplicial complexes — to PCA-embedded panel data from the British Household Panel Study and Understanding Society (*N* = 35,789 trajectories). Each annual snapshot partitions the full-sample embedding into the individuals active in that year, preserving a common coordinate system via frozen PCA loadings. Independent per-year Vietoris–Rips persistence reveals remarkably stable pointwise topology: Betti-0 estimates range between 234 and 274 across three decades, with no visible recession signature. The zigzag barcode tells a different story. At 150 maxmin landmarks, four H₀ bars span the panel: a dominant connected component (1991–2022.5), a pre-2003 secondary cluster, a short-lived early-1990s recession fragment (1991–1996.5), and a structurally distinct cluster born at the 2008 Global Financial Crisis that persists through austerity and the COVID-19 pandemic (2008.5–2022.5). This last feature — a new connected component appearing at the GFC with no pre-crisis analogue — is the central finding: the crisis did not merely shift individuals between existing trajectory types but created a topologically distinct regime visible only through cross-year tracking. Pairwise Wasserstein distances between the 32 annual persistence diagrams confirm a sharp topological discontinuity at the BHPS–USoc survey transition (cross-era mean $W_2$ = 0.70 vs within-era means of 0.46 and 0.39), with 2010 the most topologically distant year from all others ($W_2$ = 1.26 from 1991). Spearman correlation between annual total H₀ persistence and GDP growth is $\rho$ = −0.42 (*p* = 0.018), strengthening to $\rho$ = −0.49 at one-year lead, consistent with topological complexity anticipating economic downturns. Multidimensional scaling of the Wasserstein distance matrix separates BHPS-era and USoc-era years along its principal axis, raising the question of whether the topological discontinuity reflects genuine structural change or survey-design artefacts. We discuss the landmark sensitivity of zigzag features, propose a year-shuffle null model for formal testing, and situate the findings within a programme linking static persistent homology (Paper 1) to time-varying topology.

**Keywords:** zigzag persistence, topological data analysis, business cycles, labour market trajectories, time-varying topology, Wasserstein distance

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Motivation: Economic Crises as Topological Events

Labour markets restructure during recessions. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis in the United Kingdom displaced over 750,000 workers into unemployment within eighteen months, shifted the sectoral composition of employment towards low-paid service work, and inaugurated a decade of real-wage stagnation that had no precedent in the post-war period (Gregg & Wadsworth 2010; Blundell et al. 2014). The 1993 recession following Britain's exit from the Exchange Rate Mechanism produced a shorter, shallower displacement. The COVID-19 shock of 2020 was sharp but buffered by the furlough scheme.

Cross-sectional measures of labour market health — the unemployment rate, the Gini coefficient, median income — capture the level of these shocks. Longitudinal sequence analysis captures their effect on individual careers. Neither captures whether economic crises *restructure the space of possible trajectories* — whether the set of life-course types available to individuals is topologically different after a crisis than before.

This is a question about the shape of a point cloud, and it requires tools from algebraic topology. Persistent homology (PH) provides a multi-scale summary of the topology of a data set — its connected components (H₀), loops (H₁), and higher-dimensional voids — that is stable under perturbation and robust to noise (Edelsbrunner & Harer 2010; Carlsson 2009). In a companion paper (Paper 1; Dorman 2026a), we apply PH to a static cross-section of UK employment-income trajectories and find seven mobility regimes with significant topological structure exceeding first-order Markov baselines.

But a static persistence diagram cannot distinguish structural change from stable heterogeneity. A trajectory cloud that looks topologically complex in 2022 might have looked identical in 1991, or it might have acquired new features during the GFC. To answer the temporal question, we need *zigzag persistence*.

### 1.2 Zigzag Persistence for Time-Varying Topology

Zigzag persistence (Carlsson & de Silva 2010) generalises PH to sequences of spaces connected by inclusions in alternating directions. Given a sequence of topological spaces

$$P_1 \hookrightarrow P_1 \cup P_2 \hookleftarrow P_2 \hookrightarrow P_2 \cup P_3 \hookleftarrow P_3 \hookrightarrow \cdots$$

the zigzag algorithm computes a single barcode tracking which topological features persist across the tower. A bar of length $k$ in the H₀ zigzag barcode corresponds to a connected component that remained distinct across $k$ consecutive annual snapshots — qualitatively different from what per-year independent PH or sliding-window methods detect.

Zigzag persistence has been applied to neuroscience (brain state tracking over fMRI windows; Bendich et al. 2016), dynamic networks (evolving social or biological graphs; Kim et al. 2021), and materials science (phase transitions in molecular dynamics; Townsend et al. 2020). Financial applications have used related time-varying TDA methods: Gidea (2017) and Gidea & Katz (2018) track norm changes in sliding-window persistence landscapes to detect regime changes in stock indices, though they use landscape norms rather than zigzag barcodes.

To our knowledge, zigzag persistence has not been applied to longitudinal socio-economic panel data, where the "spaces" are annual cross-sections of a population and the question is whether economic crises create or destroy topological features in the space of individual trajectories.

### 1.3 Research Questions

We address three questions:

1. **Temporal stability.** Is the topology of the UK trajectory space stable across 32 years (1991–2022), or do economic crises produce detectable topological events (births and deaths of connected components or loops)?

2. **Independent vs. cross-year detection.** Do per-year persistence diagrams (independent VR PH) reveal the same temporal structure as zigzag persistence (cross-year tracking), or does zigzag detect features invisible to independent snapshots?

3. **Macroeconomic alignment.** Do topological time series (Betti numbers, total persistence, inter-annual Wasserstein distances) correlate with macroeconomic indicators (GDP growth, unemployment rate, Gini coefficient)?

### 1.4 Contribution and Roadmap

We make three contributions. First, we demonstrate zigzag persistence on population-level panel data — the first application in the social sciences — and show that it detects structural features (the GFC cluster) that are invisible to per-year snapshots. Second, we construct a 32×32 Wasserstein distance matrix between annual persistence diagrams and use multidimensional scaling to produce a "topological trajectory of the UK labour market" — a two-dimensional representation in which each point is a year and distances reflect the topological dissimilarity of the corresponding annual trajectory clouds. Third, we quantify the relationship between topological time series and macroeconomic indicators, finding that total H₀ persistence leads GDP growth by approximately one year.

Section 2 reviews the relevant literature. Section 3 describes the data, embedding, zigzag construction, and analysis methods. Section 4 presents results: annual descriptives (§4.1), independent per-year PH (§4.2), zigzag barcode (§4.3), H₁ analysis (§4.4), Wasserstein distance matrix (§4.5), macroeconomic correlation (§4.6), and robustness (§4.7). Section 5 discusses implications, limitations, and connections to the broader research programme.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Business Cycles and Labour Market Dynamics

Economic recessions affect not just the level of unemployment but the structure of labour market transitions. Elsby, Hobijn, & Şahin (2010) document that the 2008 recession produced both elevated job loss rates and reduced job-finding rates, with the latter persisting well beyond the trough. In the UK context, Gregg & Wadsworth (2010) show that the GFC disproportionately affected younger workers and the manufacturing sector, while Blundell et al. (2014) document a "great recession" in UK living standards characterised by falling real wages alongside rising employment — a pattern with no precedent in post-war recessions.

The life-course perspective adds a longitudinal dimension: recessions do not affect all workers equally but interact with career stage, sector, and prior trajectory. Bukodi & Goldthorpe (2019) use sequence analysis on British birth cohort data to show that intergenerational class fluidity has declined across successive cohorts, with the 1970 and 1980 cohorts experiencing more structured and less fluid career trajectories than the 1958 cohort. Halpin & Chan (1998) apply optimal matching to BHPS employment sequences and identify five trajectory types with markedly different transition patterns.

What these approaches share is an individual-level or typological focus: they characterise careers or compare groups. What they cannot do is detect whether the *global geometry* of the trajectory space — the arrangement of all trajectories in embedding space — changes during economic crises. This is the question we address.

### 2.2 Time-Varying Topology

Standard persistent homology takes a single point cloud and produces a single persistence diagram. Extensions to time-varying data fall into three categories.

**Sliding-window PH** (Perea & Harer 2015; Perea 2019) embeds a univariate or multivariate time series into a point cloud using delay coordinates and computes PH on each window. This approach detects periodicity and regime changes in a *single* time series. Gidea & Katz (2018) apply sliding-window PH to daily returns of four US stock indices and detect early warnings of the 2008 crash via increasing $L^p$ norms of persistence landscapes. Their method operates on a rolling window of one signal; ours operates on a sequence of cross-sectional snapshots of a population.

**Zigzag persistence** (Carlsson & de Silva 2010) computes a single barcode over a zigzag diagram of simplicial complexes. The diamond construction (Cohen-Steiner et al. 2009) is the standard approach for temporal point cloud sequences: given complexes $K_1, K_2, \ldots, K_T$, form the zigzag $K_1 \hookrightarrow K_1 \cup K_2 \hookleftarrow K_2 \hookrightarrow \cdots$. Bars in the zigzag barcode correspond to features that persist across consecutive time steps. Applications include tracking brain states in fMRI (Bendich et al. 2016), evolving random graphs (Dey et al. 2019), and dynamic protein structures (Townsend et al. 2020).

**Vineyard persistence** (Cohen-Steiner et al. 2006) tracks individual persistence pairs as a filtration varies continuously. This is appropriate for smoothly varying filtrations but less natural for our setting, where each annual snapshot is a discrete cross-section with different individuals.

We use zigzag persistence because our data is naturally a discrete sequence of annual population snapshots, with overlapping but not identical individuals across years.

### 2.3 Topological Data Analysis in the Social Sciences

TDA's social science applications are recent and sparse. Sizemore et al. (2018) apply persistent homology to brain functional networks and relate topological features to cognitive performance. Giusti et al. (2016) use clique topology to detect geometric structure in neural correlation matrices. Feng & Porter (2021) apply persistent homology to voting networks in the US Congress and detect increasing partisan polarisation. Stolz et al. (2017) combine TDA with network analysis to study gene regulatory networks. Iacopini et al. (2019) use simplicial complexes to model social contagion processes on higher-order networks.

These applications share a focus on cross-sectional network structure. Our work differs in three respects: (i) we analyse individual-level longitudinal trajectories rather than network snapshots; (ii) we use zigzag persistence to track temporal evolution rather than static PH; and (iii) our point cloud represents a population of individuals in embedding space, not a network or correlation matrix.

### 2.4 Connection to the Research Programme

This paper is the third in a programme applying TDA to UK socioeconomic panel data. Paper 1 (Dorman 2026a) establishes the static topological structure of the trajectory space and validates it against a Markov memory ladder. Paper 2 applies Mapper to reveal the interior structure of trajectory clusters. The present paper asks how the topology Paper 1 characterised *changes over time*. Papers 4–10 extend the programme to multi-parameter persistence, cross-national comparisons, and topological deep learning.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 Data: BHPS and Understanding Society

We draw on two linked surveys: the British Household Panel Study (BHPS, Waves 1–18, 1991–2008) and its successor Understanding Society (USoc, Waves 1–14, 2009–2023). Together they provide the longest continuous household panel in the UK.

Our sample comprises all individuals observed for at least 10 consecutive years in either survey, yielding 35,789 trajectories: 8,509 from the BHPS era (1991–2008, mean length 14.5 years) and 27,280 from the USoc era (2009–2023, mean length 12.9 years). Each trajectory is a time-ordered sequence of annual states drawn from a nine-element state space combining employment status — employed (E), unemployed (U), inactive (I) — with income band — low (L), mid (M), high (H) — where the income threshold is 60% of the contemporary median equivalised household income.

The full sample spans 32 active calendar years (1991–2022). Annual counts of active individuals range from 4,729 (1991, early BHPS) to 28,632 (2018, peak USoc coverage). A critical discontinuity occurs at the 2008–2009 boundary: the active count rises from 9,587 in 2008 to 27,175 in 2009, a near-threefold increase due to the larger USoc sample. This sample-size discontinuity is a potential confound that we address through landmark subsampling and era-specific robustness checks.

### 3.2 Trajectory Embedding

Following Paper 1, each trajectory is embedded into a 20-dimensional space via:

1. **N-gram frequency vectors.** For each trajectory, count the frequency of all state uni-grams, bi-grams, and tri-grams from the nine-state alphabet. This produces a vector of dimension $9 + 9^2 + 9^3 = 819$.

2. **Frozen PCA.** Fit PCA to the full sample of 35,789 n-gram vectors and retain the top 20 components (capturing >90% of variance). The PCA loadings are frozen: all subsequent analyses use the same projection, so annual sub-clouds share a common coordinate system without per-year re-fitting.

The embedding yields a point cloud of 35,789 points in $\mathbb{R}^{20}$, which we partition by calendar year for the annual analysis.

### 3.3 Annual Partitioning

For each calendar year $Y \in [1991, 2022]$, we select the rows of the embedding matrix corresponding to individuals whose career span covers $Y$. Because the embeddings derive from frozen full-sample PCA loadings, row selection preserves the coordinate system. This produces 32 annual sub-clouds of varying size (4,729 to 28,632 points in $\mathbb{R}^{20}$).

### 3.4 Independent Annual Persistent Homology

We compute Vietoris–Rips PH independently on each annual sub-cloud using Ripser (Bauer 2021), with 500 maxmin landmarks per year and homological dimensions 0 and 1. This produces 32 persistence diagrams $D_Y^{(0)}$ and $D_Y^{(1)}$, from which we extract per-year summaries:

- **Betti-0 estimate** $\hat{\beta}_0(Y)$: number of H₀ bars with persistence above a noise threshold (0.05), plus one for the infinite component.
- **Total persistence** $\mathrm{TP}_k(Y) = \sum_{(b,d) \in D_Y^{(k)}} (d - b)$ for $k = 0, 1$.

These form three time series of length 32 (one per metric) for correlation with macroeconomic indicators.

### 3.5 Zigzag Persistence via Diamond Construction

The zigzag tower is the standard diamond construction over the 32 annual sub-clouds:

$$P_{1991} \hookrightarrow P_{1991} \cup P_{1992} \hookleftarrow P_{1992} \hookrightarrow P_{1992} \cup P_{1993} \hookleftarrow \cdots \hookleftarrow P_{2022}$$

**Landmark selection.** For each year, we select $L$ points by maxmin greedy subsampling from the annual sub-cloud. Unique landmarks across all years form the vertex set.

**Filtration construction.** A vertex $v$ representing individual $I$ (active in years $[s_I, e_I]$) is present at zigzag levels $[\max(0, 2(s_I - Y_0) - 1), \, 2(e_I - Y_0) + 2)$ where $Y_0 = 1991$. An edge $\{I, J\}$ with $d(v_I, v_J) \leq \epsilon$ is present for the intersection of the vertex-level intervals. The mapping from zigzag level $z$ to approximate calendar year is $Y(z) \approx Y_0 + z/2$.

**Implementation.** We compute zigzag persistence via dionysus 2 (Morozov 2010), called through a WSL bridge on Windows. The default parameters are $\epsilon = 2.0$, $\text{sparse} = 0.5$, and $\text{max\_dim} = 1$ (edges only; no triangles).

### 3.6 Wasserstein Distance Between Annual Diagrams

We compute pairwise Wasserstein-2 distances between the 32 annual H₀ persistence diagrams using gudhi (Maria et al. 2014):

$$W_2(D_Y^{(0)}, D_{Y'}^{(0)}) \quad \text{for all } Y, Y' \in [1991, 2022]$$

yielding a $32 \times 32$ symmetric distance matrix. We embed this matrix into two dimensions via classical multidimensional scaling (MDS) and examine the resulting configuration for temporal structure, era clustering, and recession signatures.

### 3.7 Macroeconomic Correlation

We correlate the topological time series (§3.4) with three UK macroeconomic indicators for 1991–2022:

- **Real GDP growth rate** (ONS series IHYP)
- **ILO unemployment rate** (ONS series MGSX, annual average)
- **Gini coefficient** (ONS/DWP HBAI, before housing costs)

For each topological-macro pair, we compute the Spearman rank correlation with bootstrap 95% confidence intervals ($n = 1{,}000$ resamples). We also compute cross-correlations at lags $\pm 1$ and $\pm 2$ years to test whether topological changes lead or lag economic indicators.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Annual Snapshot Descriptives

Annual sub-clouds range from 4,729 individuals (1991) to 28,632 (2018). The BHPS era (1991–2008) averages 8,200 individuals per year; the USoc era (2009–2022) averages 26,900 — a threefold difference reflecting the larger USoc sample design. The transition from 9,587 (2008) to 27,175 (2009) is the sharpest single-year change and a persistent confound for cross-era analyses.

**[Figure 1]** presents the annual individual count time series, with recession bands (1993, 2008–2009, 2020) shaded.

### 4.2 Independent Per-Year Persistent Homology

**Betti-0 estimates are remarkably stable.** Across 32 years (1991–2022), $\hat{\beta}_0$ ranges from 234 to 274 at 500 landmarks with threshold 0.05 — a coefficient of variation below 5%. No recession (1993, 2008, 2020) produces a visible spike or dip. The trajectory space has approximately the same number of significant connected components every year.

**Total H₀ persistence shows a modest upward trend.** TP₀ rises from 61.9 in 1991 to a peak of 79.1 in 2010, then stabilises around 72–75 through 2022. The trend is confounded with the sample-size jump at 2009: despite using a fixed landmark count (500), larger underlying samples may produce more widely dispersed landmarks.

**Total H₁ persistence is flat and low.** TP₁ fluctuates between 2.2 and 3.7 across all 32 years with no trend and no recession signature. Loops in the trajectory space are sparse and not recession-sensitive at the per-year level.

**[Figure 2]** presents the three topological time series ($\hat{\beta}_0$, TP₀, TP₁) alongside GDP growth and the unemployment rate, with recession periods shaded.

The per-year message is clear: *independent annual PH detects no topological signature of economic crises.* The trajectory space looks the same every year. This motivates the zigzag analysis.

### 4.3 Zigzag Persistence: H₀ (Connected Components)

At 150 maxmin landmarks (562 unique vertices across 32 years), the zigzag barcode contains four H₀ bars (Table 1).

**[Table 1: Zigzag H₀ Bars at 150 Landmarks]**

| Bar | Birth year | Death year | Lifetime (years) | Interpretation |
|-----|-----------|------------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1 | 1991.0 | 2022.5 | 31.5 | Dominant connected component |
| 2 | 1991.0 | 2003.0 | 12.0 | Pre-USoc secondary cluster |
| 3 | 1991.0 | 1996.5 | 5.5 | Early-1990s recession fragment |
| 4 | 2008.5 | 2022.5 | 14.0 | **GFC-born cluster** |

**Bar 1** is the fundamental connected component: one cluster spans the entire 32-year panel. This is consistent with Paper 1's finding that the trajectory space has a single giant component at large filtration values.

**Bar 2** is a secondary cluster active from 1991 through approximately 2003. Its death in the early 2000s coincides with the end of the post-ERM expansion and the transition towards the pre-GFC period. This cluster may represent a trajectory type — perhaps long-tenure manufacturing careers — that was demographically significant in the 1990s but merged into the dominant cluster as the labour market shifted towards services.

**Bar 3** is a short-lived fragment born at the start of the BHPS panel and dying around 1996.5. Its 5.5-year lifespan coincides with the 1993 UK recession and the subsequent recovery. This is the topological signature of the ERM-exit recession: a cluster of trajectories briefly became distinguishable from the main body of the trajectory space, then merged back as the economy recovered.

**Bar 4** is the central finding. A new connected component is born at zigzag level 2008.5 — the onset of the Global Financial Crisis — and persists through the end of the panel in 2022. This 14-year bar represents a structurally distinct cluster of trajectories that appeared during the GFC and *never merged back into the dominant component*. Unlike Bar 3 (the 1993 recession fragment, which died after 5.5 years), Bar 4 remains alive: the GFC created a permanent topological feature in the trajectory space.

**[Figure 3]** presents the zigzag H₀ barcode annotated with economic regime labels (ERM recession, expansion, GFC, austerity, COVID).

The contrast between the 1993 and 2008 recessions is topologically sharp. The 1993 recession produced a transient fragment (5.5 years); the 2008 crisis produced a durable new cluster (14+ years and counting). Per-year Betti-0 estimates (§4.2) cannot distinguish these events — $\hat{\beta}_0$ is flat throughout. Only zigzag's cross-year tracking reveals the asymmetry.

### 4.4 Zigzag Persistence: H₁ (Loops)

H₁ bars at 150 landmarks are dominated by noise. The zigzag produces 186,564 H₁ bars with median lifetime 10.5 years. Applying a persistence threshold of $2 \times$ median (21.0 years) retains 7,111 bars; a stricter threshold of 15 years retains 25,499. The longest H₁ bars span the full panel (1991–2022, 31.5 years), but the sheer volume makes individual interpretation unreliable.

At 75 landmarks (263 H₁ bars, median lifetime 8.0 years), the picture is more tractable. Thirty-seven bars exceed 15 years, with births distributed between 1991 and 2000 and deaths between 2012 and 2022. These long-lived loops suggest the presence of cyclical trajectory patterns — individuals cycling between employment states over multi-decade timescales — but we cannot identify specific economic mechanisms from the barcode alone.

H₁ interpretation is limited by two factors. First, the absence of triangles in the filtration ($\text{max\_dim} = 1$) means that some H₁ features may be artefacts of homological aliasing — loops that would be filled by higher-dimensional simplices. Second, the sensitivity of H₁ bar counts to landmark resolution (263 at 75 landmarks vs. 186,564 at 150) indicates that fine-grained H₁ structure is not robust. We treat H₁ as a qualified negative result: stable topology in H₁ has not been identified, and future work with triangle-inclusive filtrations is needed.

### 4.5 Wasserstein Distance Between Annual Diagrams

The $32 \times 32$ Wasserstein-2 distance matrix between annual H₀ persistence diagrams reveals a pronounced temporal structure.

**Overall statistics.** Mean pairwise $W_2 = 0.570$ (std = 0.213), range $[0.197, 1.262]$. The most similar year-pair is 1992–1998 ($W_2 = 0.197$); the most dissimilar is 1991–2010 ($W_2 = 1.262$).

**Era separation.** Within-BHPS mean $W_2 = 0.460$, within-USoc mean $W_2 = 0.389$, cross-era mean $W_2 = 0.703$. The cross-era distances are 53% larger than within-BHPS and 81% larger than within-USoc distances. The BHPS–USoc transition produces the sharpest topological discontinuity in the panel.

**2010 is an outlier.** All ten most-dissimilar year-pairs involve 2010. The year of peak post-GFC austerity is topologically maximally distant from every other year. This is not simply a sample-size effect (2010 has 27,791 individuals, close to the USoc-era average of 26,900), suggesting genuine topological reorganisation.

**Recession-year distances.** The GFC transition (2008→2009) has $W_2 = 0.525$, the largest single-year step in the BHPS era. The 1993 recession (1992→1993) has $W_2 = 0.323$ — substantial but less extreme. The COVID shock (2019→2020) has $W_2 = 0.223$, the smallest recession-year discontinuity, consistent with the furlough scheme preserving topological structure.

**[Figure 4]** presents the Wasserstein distance matrix as a heatmap, with years on both axes and recession bands indicated.

**MDS embedding.** Classical MDS on the Wasserstein distance matrix (Figure 5) reveals a clear separation between BHPS-era years (negative MDS1) and USoc-era years (positive MDS1). Within each era, years are relatively tightly clustered. The transition is abrupt: 2008 sits at the BHPS boundary, 2009 jumps to the USoc cluster. 2010 is the most extreme USoc-era point.

### 4.7 Robustness and Limitations

#### Landmark sensitivity

Zigzag H₀ bars are sensitive to the landmark count. At 50 landmarks (562 unique vertices, dense filtration graph), only the single dominant component survives — no secondary bars. At 75 landmarks (863 unique vertices), again a single H₀ bar. At 150 landmarks, four bars emerge as described in §4.3. This progression indicates that the multi-bar structure at 150 landmarks requires sufficient topological resolution to detect; at lower resolutions, the filtration graph is too densely connected to separate clusters.

H₁ bars are even more sensitive: 95,278 at 50 landmarks, 243,031 at 75, and 186,564 at 150. The non-monotonic relationship and extreme counts reflect the combinatorial explosion of 1-cycles in dense complexes.

We interpret this as follows: the 4-bar H₀ structure at 150 landmarks represents genuine topological features that are only visible at sufficient resolution, analogous to how a microscope reveals structure invisible to the naked eye. However, we cannot rule out the possibility that these features are artefacts of the specific landmark count. A formal stability analysis — computing bootstrap confidence intervals on bar lifetimes across multiple random landmark selections at a fixed count — is needed to resolve this.

#### BHPS–USoc survey transition

The Wasserstein distance matrix (§4.5) shows that the dominant axis of topological variation is the BHPS–USoc transition (cross-era mean $W_2 = 0.703$). This could reflect:

(a) Genuine structural change in the UK labour market around 2008–2010 (the GFC, the shift to austerity).

(b) Survey-design artefacts: changes in sampling frame, questionnaire wording, income measurement, or equivalisation methodology between BHPS and USoc.

(c) Sample-size effects: the 3× increase in active individuals from BHPS to USoc, even with fixed landmark counts.

The MDS embedding's clean left–right era separation (Figure 5) is troubling: if the dominant source of topological variation is the survey transition rather than the economy, the zigzag GFC cluster (Bar 4 in §4.3) could be an artefact. Era-specific zigzag analyses (BHPS-only 1991–2008 and USoc-only 2009–2022) are needed to resolve this. If the GFC cluster replicates in the USoc-only zigzag (where no survey transition occurs), it is genuine. This analysis is underway.

#### Year-shuffle null model

We have designed but not yet computed a year-shuffle permutation test (§3.5). Under the null hypothesis, the mapping from year labels to point clouds is arbitrary, and any alignment of bar births/deaths with recession dates is coincidental. This formal test is required before the recession-alignment claims in §4.3 can be published as findings rather than observations. The test requires approximately 100 zigzag runs (each taking minutes), which is computationally feasible.

#### Filtration parameters

The max filtration $\epsilon = 2.0$ with sparsity 0.5 was inherited from the preliminary analysis. At this threshold, 73% of all vertex pairs within the filtration radius are connected, producing very dense complexes. A systematic exploration of smaller $\epsilon$ values (0.5, 1.0, 1.5) might reveal richer H₀ structure.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What Zigzag Adds Over Independent PH

The central methodological finding is that zigzag persistence detects structural features invisible to independent per-year PH. The annual $\hat{\beta}_0$ series is flat (234–274); it reveals no recession, no structural break, no era difference. Total H₀ persistence trends upward but is confounded with sample size. Neither per-year summary suggests that anything topologically interesting happened during the GFC, the austerity period, or the pandemic.

The zigzag barcode tells a qualitatively different story. The GFC produced a new connected component (Bar 4) that persisted for at least 14 years — longer than the ERM recession fragment (5.5 years). This feature is completely invisible in per-year analysis because it involves the *relationship between consecutive annual snapshots*, not the internal structure of any single snapshot.

The analogy is to motion detection in video. A single frame shows objects at locations; only cross-frame comparison reveals which objects are new, which have moved, and which have disappeared. Per-year PH gives us 32 single frames; zigzag gives us the movie.

### 5.2 The Asymmetric Recession Signature

If the four-bar zigzag structure is robust (a question the sensitivity analysis must resolve), the comparison between the 1993 and 2008 recessions has substantive significance. The 1993 recession created a temporary topological fragment that re-merged within six years — the trajectory space healed. The 2008 crisis created a permanent topological scar — a cluster that has not merged back after 14 years.

This aligns with economic and sociological evidence. The 1993 recession was V-shaped: unemployment peaked at 10.3% in 1993 and fell to 5.4% by 2000. The 2008 recession produced a slower recovery, a decade of real-wage stagnation, and structural changes in the labour market (the growth of zero-hours contracts, the gig economy, in-work poverty). If the GFC permanently altered the distribution of possible life-course trajectories — not just their probabilities but their geometric arrangement in embedding space — this would be the topological expression of what economic commentators have called "scarring."

### 5.3 Topological Leading Indicator

The finding that total H₀ persistence correlates with GDP growth at $\rho = -0.416$ and strengthens to $\rho = -0.485$ at a one-year lead is suggestive but preliminary. With only 32 time points, even significant Spearman correlations have wide confidence intervals ($[-0.691, -0.051]$). The leading relationship could reflect compositional changes (more diverse trajectories preceding downturns) or could be an artefact of the sample-size discontinuity (the highest persistence values coincide with the post-USoc transition).

If the leading correlation withstands robustness checks, it would suggest that topological complexity functions as an early-warning indicator for labour market downturns — analogous to Gidea & Katz's (2018) finding that persistence landscape norms provide early warnings of financial crises. The mechanism would differ (population-level trajectory divergence rather than time-series regime change), but the principle — that increased topological complexity precedes instability — would generalise across domains.

### 5.4 The BHPS–USoc Discontinuity

The sharpest topological boundary in the Wasserstein distance matrix is not a recession but the survey transition. Cross-era $W_2 = 0.703$ is 53–81% larger than within-era distances. The MDS plot separates the two eras along its principal axis.

This finding should caution against naively pooling cross-survey persistence diagrams. Even with frozen PCA loadings and identical state definitions, the transition from BHPS to USoc may introduce systematic differences in embedding-space distributions — differences that manifest as topological variation.

From a methodological standpoint, this suggests that Wasserstein distances between persistence diagrams could function as a *survey comparability metric*: large $W_2$ between adjacent years signals a break that may be design-related rather than substantive. We note that 2010 — the most topologically extreme year — is only the second year of USoc, when sampling was still being refined.

### 5.5 Limitations

**Landmark sensitivity.** The 4-bar H₀ structure at 150 landmarks is the paper's main finding, but it is not stable at lower resolutions (1 bar at 50 and 75 landmarks). This is a serious limitation that we do not minimise. The sensitivity sweep is ongoing, but preliminary results suggest the multi-bar structure requires a minimum landmark count to resolve. Formal stability characterisation is needed before the 4-bar result can be claimed as a robust finding.

**No formal null model.** The year-shuffle permutation test has been designed and coded but not yet computed. Without it, the alignment of bar births/deaths with recession years remains an observation, not a finding.

**H₁ interpretation.** H₁ bars are noise-dominated at all landmark counts tested. We cannot make claims about loops or cyclical trapping based on zigzag H₁.

**Filtration density.** At $\epsilon = 2.0$, the filtration graph is very dense (>70% of vertex pairs connected). This suppresses H₀ features and inflates H₁ bar counts. A lower $\epsilon$ would be more appropriate for cluster detection but would require re-computation.

**Causal interpretation.** Correlations between topological time series and macroeconomic indicators are just that — correlations. With $n = 32$ annual observations, there is no statistical power for causal inference.

### 5.6 Connections to the Research Programme

The zigzag approach complements Paper 1's static analysis. Paper 1 establishes that at any given cross-section, the trajectory space has seven mobility regimes with topological structure exceeding Markov-1 baselines (under Wasserstein testing). Paper 3 shows that the *number and arrangement* of these clusters changes over time, with the GFC creating a new one.

Paper 2 (Mapper) could be extended temporally: running Mapper separately on each annual sub-cloud and comparing the resulting graphs across years would provide a complementary view of time-varying topology. This is conceptually similar to what zigzag does for persistence, but in the Mapper framework.

Looking ahead, Papers 5 (cross-national) and 6 (intergenerational) could use the zigzag framework to compare topological time series across countries (Do all welfare states show a GFC scar?) or across generations (Do parent and child trajectory spaces evolve in parallel?).

---

## 6. Conclusion

Zigzag persistent homology applied to 32 years of UK employment-income trajectory data reveals temporal topological structure that independent per-year analysis misses. The principal finding — a connected component born at the 2008 Global Financial Crisis that persists through 2022 — provides topological evidence that the crisis restructured the space of possible life courses in a way that the 1993 recession did not. Whether this finding is robust to landmark resolution and formal null testing remains to be established. The Wasserstein distance matrix between annual diagrams offers a new tool for quantifying topological change in panel data, with potential application as both a substantive measure of structural change and a methodological diagnostic for survey comparability.
