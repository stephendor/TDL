## Abstract

UK employment-income trajectories from Understanding Society (2009–2023, *n* = 27,280) and BHPS (1991–2008, *n* = 8,509) are embedded as n-gram frequency vectors in a 20-dimensional PCA space and analysed using Vietoris–Rips persistent homology and the Mapper algorithm. Seven mobility regimes emerge from Gaussian mixture modelling, ranging from Secure High-Employment (27.0%, stability 0.78) to Low-Income Churn (7.6%, stability 0.29). Persistent H₀ topology encodes regime separation that a globally estimated first-order Markov chain cannot reproduce when diagrams are compared as geometric objects ($W_2$-based test, $p = 0.002$), even though it does reproduce it when persistence is collapsed to a scalar ($p = 1.000$). This discrepancy — replicated on BHPS-era trajectories — establishes that test-statistic choice determines whether trajectories look Markovian. Mapper analysis deepens the regime picture: within Low-Income Churn, 80 DBSCAN sub-regions span a four-unit income-gradient range, and nodes near the Employed Mid boundary show employment rates of 0.7 with final income trending to mid-range, while nodes near Inactive Low show rates below 0.3. Escape from disadvantaged regimes is rare (5.6% overall; 17.8% for working-age individuals), with initial regime stronger than parental class origin as a predictor. These findings resist sequence-analytic explanations: optimal matching at matched $k = 7$ yields ARI = 0.26 against the topological regime solution, capturing broad contours but missing within-regime geometry.

**Keywords:** persistent homology, topological data analysis, Mapper algorithm, social mobility, career inequality, life-course trajectories, Wasserstein distance, mobility regimes

## §1 Introduction

The United Kingdom has one of the weakest records of intergenerational social mobility among comparable welfare states, and the pattern has worsened across cohorts born since the 1960s (Blanden et al. 2004; Goldthorpe & Mills 2008). What drives this stagnation is not captured well by the statistics that usually describe it. Transition rate matrices and sequence-analytic typologies document what states people pass through and which they cluster into, but they do not characterise the *global structure* of the trajectory space: how many distinct career regimes exist, how sharply they are separated, whether movement within them is organised by persistent sub-populations with different prospects, or whether the stickiness of disadvantage sits at the level of individual trajectories or of structural attractors in the space of all trajectories.

This paper asks those questions about UK employment-income careers using topological data analysis (TDA). The point of entry is not a commitment to topology as a preferred method but a specific limitation of existing approaches. Markov chain models characterise local transition probabilities; they cannot detect whether the global configuration of all trajectories has a shape that a Markov process would generate, or whether that shape carries regime-specific substructure not visible in a globally estimated transition matrix. Sequence analysis methods group trajectories by pairwise distance; they have no notion of topological invariants such as connected components or loops, and no standard framework for testing whether observed clustering exceeds what null-model baselines would produce.

Persistent homology addresses both limitations. It identifies topological features — connected components (H₀), loops (H₁), higher-dimensional voids — that persist across scales in the embedded trajectory space. Crucially, it supports formal hypothesis testing through structured null-model batteries. The null-model design we use here — a Markov memory ladder of five increasingly structured null hypotheses — reveals a finding that would be invisible to any single test: the choice of test statistic determines whether first-order Markov dynamics are judged sufficient. Total persistence (a scalar summary) says they are. Wasserstein diagram distances (comparing the full geometric configuration of persistence features) say they are not ($p = 0.002$). Both statements are mathematically correct about different aspects of the comparison; the tension between them is the methodologically important finding, and it has consequences for any TDA analysis of sequential data.

The Mapper algorithm provides a complementary view from the inside. Where persistent homology characterises the global shape of the trajectory space, Mapper resolves local geometry within each regime, revealing sub-populations with detectably different labour-market prospects that regime-level statistics collapse to a single mean. This within-regime anatomy is not a secondary finding: the policy implication is that individuals labelled Low-Income Churn are not a homogeneous group. Some sit near the boundary with stable employment; others near the boundary with absorbing inactivity. These sub-populations plausibly require different interventions, and Mapper identifies where in the trajectory space the distinction matters most.

The paper proceeds as follows. §2 situates the work in the stratification, mobility, and life-course literatures. §3 describes the data, embedding, and methods. §4 presents global topology results and the seven-regime typology. §5 develops the within-regime anatomy using Mapper. §6 reports stratified analyses by gender, parental class, and birth cohort, and cross-survey replication on BHPS-era trajectories. §7 discusses implications for the study of career inequality and the methodology of topological hypothesis testing.

## §2 Background

### §2.1 Inequality, Trajectories, and Structural Absorptivity

A growing body of work treats intergenerational inequality not as a single-point comparison between parents and children but as a property of the full trajectory through the employment-income space over the working life (Goldthorpe & Mills 2008; Bukodi & Goldthorpe 2019). This tradition distinguishes between short-run disruption — a period of unemployment or income loss from which recovery is rapid — and longer-run structural absorptivity, where disadvantaged states generate momentum that makes exit genuinely rare. Jenkins (2011) documented extensive income churning in UK panel data: many individuals cycle between poverty and near-poverty for years without achieving durable exits. Vandecasteele (2010) distinguished "recurrent" from "transient" poverty trajectories in Belgian data, showing that the two require different theoretical accounts. Savage et al. (2013) showed that contemporary social class in Britain is stratified on cultural and social capital in ways that reinforce economic disadvantage.

The theoretical framework relevant here is that of structural mobility regimes: the idea that the labour market does not produce a single gradual income gradient but multiple quasi-stable configurations between which movement is inhibited by institutional, informational, and human capital barriers. This is related to the economics of poverty traps (Azariadis & Stachurski 2005; Barrett & Carter 2013), where nonlinear returns to investment or threshold dynamics create multiple equilibria — though the analogy is not precise, because the relevant equilibria here are dynamic attractors in trajectory space rather than fixed points in a production function. The empirical question is whether such regime-like structure exists in the UK data, how stable it is within and across careers, and whether the mechanisms responsible show up in the geometry of the trajectory manifold rather than only in aggregate transition rates.

Gender remains central to the structure of labour market inequality in the UK. The employment rate gap has narrowed since the 1980s but inactivity driven by caring responsibilities falls disproportionately on women (Kan & Laurie 2018). Birth cohort interacts with institutional change: welfare reform, labour market deregulation, and changes to the pension age all produced differential effects across cohorts that should be visible in the trajectory topology. Parental class (NS-SEC) predicts both employment security and income levels in adulthood (Blanden et al. 2004), though the mechanisms run partly through occupational inheritance and partly through human capital accumulation.

### §2.2 Sequence Analysis and Its Limitations

Sequence analysis has been the dominant framework for life-course research since Abbott's (1995) methodological programme. Optimal matching (OM) and its variants compute pairwise distances between state sequences, producing distance matrices that support hierarchical and partitional clustering into trajectory "types" (Abbott & Tsay 2000; Lesnard 2010; Gauthier et al. 2010; Studer & Ritschard 2016). The resulting typologies have been applied to employment careers (Halpin & Chan 1998), family formation (Elzinga & Liefbroer 2007), and housing pathways (Coulter et al. 2016).

The method has known limitations for the questions posed here. First, the substitution cost matrix introduces a design choice that can determine substantive conclusions (Robette & Thibault 2008). Second, OM measures pairwise sequence dissimilarity but has no concept of the global geometry of the trajectory space — no notion of whether the resulting cluster structure is topologically connected, whether loops or voids exist, or how the clusters relate hierarchically across scales. Third, the absence of a standard null-testing framework means that the significance of any cluster solution is evaluated only by internal cohesion metrics (silhouette width, Calinski-Harabasz index), not by formal comparison to explicit generative baselines. Fourth, as Halpin (2010) showed, OM cluster solutions are typically internally heterogeneous: silhouette scores rarely exceed 0.3, but this within-cluster heterogeneity has no natural representation in the OM framework.

Topological methods complement rather than replace sequence analysis. The regime typology produced by TDA resembles what OM finds in similar data (ARI = 0.26 at $k = 7$; §4.3), but the topological approach adds: a principled metric-space embedding that supports formal geometry; null-model testing against generative Markov baselines; and — via Mapper — a spatially explicit representation of within-regime heterogeneity that OM cannot provide. These contributions are the subject of the companion methods paper (P01-B; companion paper), which develops the testing framework in full. The present paper focuses on substantive findings about UK career inequality.

### §2.3 Topological Data Analysis in Social Research

TDA has seen rapid methodological development over the past fifteen years (Carlsson 2009; Edelsbrunner & Harer 2010), with applications to financial time series (Gidea & Katz 2018), brain networks (Saggar et al. 2018), genomics (Rizvi et al. 2017), and network sociology (Sizemore et al. 2018). For life-course research, the relevant properties are: the stability of topological invariants under noise (Cohen-Steiner et al. 2007), making the analysis robust to survey measurement error and imputation; the ability to characterise structure at multiple scales simultaneously, not collapsing to a single partition; and the availability of formal significance tests via structured null models (Robinson & Turner 2017).

The Mapper algorithm (Singh, Mémoli & Carlsson 2007) provides a complementary instrument. Where persistent homology computes global topological invariants, Mapper constructs a graph-based summary that preserves local geometric structure, enabling visualisation and inspection of sub-populations within each topological regime. Outside the social sciences it has been used to identify cell-differentiation branches in RNA-seq data (Rizvi et al. 2017) and racial-profiling subgroups invisible to standard clustering (Lum et al. 2013). Life-course sociology — where the question is not merely "how many types exist?" but "where within each type do outcomes diverge?" — provides a natural domain of application, to which neither sequence analysis nor persistent homology alone is well suited.

## §3 Data and Methods

### §3.1 Data

Our primary analysis uses Understanding Society (USoc, waves 1–14, 2009–2023), a nationally representative household panel that succeeded the British Household Panel Study in 2009. From over 118,000 respondents we select individuals with at least 10 consecutive years of valid employment-income observations within the USoc window, yielding 27,280 trajectories (mean length 12.9 years, median 13, range 10–14; start years 2009–2013).

Each person-year is assigned to one of nine states crossing employment status with income band. Employment status derives from `jbstat`: employed (E) includes full-time, part-time, and self-employment; unemployed (U) follows the ILO definition; inactive (I) includes retirement, education, long-term sickness, and caring. Income bands are defined annually relative to the contemporary equivalised household income median: low (L, below 60%), mid (M, 60–120%), high (H, above 120%). The resulting state space {EL, EM, EH, UL, UM, UH, IL, IM, IH} combines labour market attachment and material standard of living in each observation year.

The sample is 56% female (14,402 women, 11,243 men; 1,635 with missing sex); birth cohorts span the 1930s through the 1990s. Parental NS-SEC is available for 60.9% of the sample (16,623 of 27,280); missingness is non-random ($\chi^2 = 147.74$, $df = 6$, $p < 10^{-29}$) and all NS-SEC-stratified results carry this caveat. Forward-fill imputation for gaps of one or two years in otherwise continuous trajectories affects 4.9% of person-years; supplementary analysis confirms that imputation rates are balanced across regimes (Supplement §S2).

For cross-survey replication we separately construct 8,509 BHPS-era trajectories (1991–2008, mean length 14.5 years) by linking BHPS person identifiers to USoc identifiers via the cross-wave identifier file, and equivalising BHPS net household income (`fihhmn`) using the modified OECD scale (`eq_moecd`). Cross-era results are reported in §6.2.

### §3.2 Embedding and Vietoris–Rips Pipeline

We embed trajectories in a continuous metric space suitable for TDA following the procedure detailed in the companion paper (P01-B); Supplement §S1 gives the full specification. Briefly: for each trajectory of length $T$ we compute unigram (9 dimensions: relative frequency of each of the nine states) and bigram (81 dimensions: relative frequency of each consecutive state pair) frequency vectors, standardise the concatenated 90-dimensional vector, and project to 20 dimensions via PCA. The resulting point cloud $X \subset \mathbb{R}^{20}$ of 27,280 points is the input to all subsequent analyses.

Vietoris–Rips persistent homology is computed using Ripser (Bauer 2021) on maxmin landmark subsets (5,000 landmarks for total-persistence tests, 2,000 for Wasserstein tests) with a filtration threshold at the 75th percentile of pairwise distances. Output comprises H₀ (connected components) and H₁ (loops) persistence diagrams $D_q(X)$ ($q = 0, 1$). Seven mobility regimes are identified by fitting Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to the PCA-20D embedding using BIC model selection across $k = 3, \ldots, 15$; BIC selects $k = 7$.

### §3.3 Null-Model Testing

Null-model testing follows the Markov memory ladder developed in the companion paper (P01-B). Five null models of increasing structural fidelity — label shuffle, cohort shuffle, order shuffle, Markov order-1, Markov order-2 — are used to decompose the topological signal. Two test statistics are used throughout: total persistence ($\sum_i (d_i - b_i)$, a scalar) and Wasserstein $W_2$ diagram distances (comparing the full geometric configuration of birth-death pairs). As we show below, the two statistics give different answers for the Markov-1 null. Full testing details are reported in the companion paper; here we state results and focus on their substantive interpretation.

### §3.4 Mapper Construction

Mapper graphs are constructed using KeplerMapper 2.1 (van Veen & Saul 2019). The lens function projects the PCA-20D embedding onto the first two principal components (PCA-2D), capturing the dominant employment-inactivity and income gradients. The cover uses $n_\text{cubes} = 30$ and overlap fraction $= 0.5$; clustering within each cover element uses DBSCAN (eps $= 0.5$, min\_samples $= 5$) as a conservative dense-region view, and agglomerative clustering (distance threshold $= 1.5$) as a complete-coverage view. The sensitivity of results across 24 configurations — two clustering algorithms, four lens functions, three parameter values each — is summarised in §5.2; the full table is in Supplement §S3.

## §4 Global Topology and Mobility Regimes

### §4.1 Descriptive Profile

The nine-state distribution is dominated by employment: EH (high-income employed) is the most common dominant state (42.1% of individuals), followed by inactive-low (13.7%), employed-mid (12.5%), inactive-mid (12.1%), and inactive-high (12.0%). Unemployment is comparatively rare as a dominant state: UL (1.1%), UM (0.5%), UH (0.4%). Low-income employment (EL) dominates 5.7% of trajectories.

[Figure 1: Trajectory heatmaps showing exemplar sequences for each of the seven regimes]

### §4.2 Persistence Diagrams

Persistent homology on the PCA-20D embedding with 5,000 maxmin landmarks reveals substantial structure in both dimensions.

**H₀ (connected components):** 5,000 features; total persistence 20,411.1; maximum persistence 15.81. The dominance of a single long-lived component (persistence 15.81, mean 4.08) indicates that most regime clusters merge at large filtration values but are well separated at smaller scales. Single-linkage behaviour in 20-dimensional Vietoris–Rips complexes yields one dominant connected component (99.98% of trajectories) plus six singleton outliers — H₀ encodes connectivity and hierarchy, not the density modes that define the GMM regimes.

**H₁ (loops):** 5,962 features; total persistence 2,224.7; maximum persistence 3.21. Loops are numerous but not deeply persistent: maximum H₁ persistence (3.21) is roughly a fifth of maximum H₀ (15.81).

[Figure 2: Persistence diagrams for H₀ and H₁, with null envelopes from order-shuffle and Markov-1 null models]

### §4.3 Null-Model Ladder: The Markov Memory Discrepancy

The five null models produce a clean result under total persistence and a different result under Wasserstein distances. Table 1 summarises both.

**Table 1: Markov Memory Ladder — Selected Results**

| Null Model | Test statistic | Dim | Result | *p* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Order shuffle | Total persistence | H₀ | Rejected | < 0.005 |
| Order shuffle | $W_2$ diagram distance | H₀ | Borderline | 0.058 |
| Markov-1 | Total persistence | H₀ | Not rejected | 1.000 |
| Markov-1 | $W_2$ diagram distance | H₀ | **Rejected** | **0.002** |
| Markov-2 | $W_2$ diagram distance | H₀ | Not rejected | 0.546 |
| Label shuffle | Both | H₀ | Not rejected (control) | > 0.31 |
| All nulls | Both | H₁ | Not rejected | ≥ 0.058 |

Under total persistence, Markov-1 surrogates generate *more* aggregate persistence than the real data (null mean H₀ = 21,138.3 vs. observed 20,411.1), yielding $p = 1.000$ — a finding that would normally lead to the conclusion that first-order dynamics fully account for the topology. The Wasserstein result overturns this: the observed $D_0(X)$ is significantly more distant from Markov-1 null diagrams than those null diagrams are from each other (mean obs-null $W_2$ = 11.42 vs. null-null $W_2$ = 5.99; $p = 0.002$).

The mechanism is clear: the globally estimated Markov transition matrix generates trajectories that are collectively *more diffuse* than the real data — more total persistence, more features — but distributes them in a different geometric configuration. The real data has regime-specific substructure: components that form at particular filtration scales and merge in a particular hierarchy. The global Markov chain, parameterised without regime-specific heterogeneity, smears this substructure out. Upgrading to second-order Markov dynamics removes the discrepancy: $W_2$ Markov-2 H₀ $p = 0.546$, localising the non-Markovian structure to two-step memory rather than to arbitrarily long path dependence.

This discrepancy is not an artefact. Label shuffle serves as a negative control in both statistics ($p > 0.31$ for both, both dimensions): the specific identity of states does not drive diagram geometry when structure is otherwise intact. Bootstrap stability analysis (10 resamples, 50 permutations each, 500 landmarks) confirms Markov-1 H₀ rejection in 10/10 draws ($p_\text{mean} = 0.000$). Intra-regime compactness under 500 order-shuffled re-embeddings confirms that all seven regimes are significantly tighter in the observed embedding than in order-shuffled versions (z-scores −30.5 to −67.9, all $p < 0.001$), with churning regimes showing the largest compactness ratios.

The methodological implication extends beyond this application: scalar persistence summaries are standard in TDA hypothesis testing but can produce false negatives when the null generates as much or more aggregate persistence while differing in diagram shape. The companion paper (P01-B) develops the formal testing framework; the substantive lesson is that the Markov memory ladder should be climbed with both test statistics.

**H₁ (loops) result.** H₁ is non-significant under both test statistics and all null models in the USoc data, including a phase-order shuffle null ($p = 0.98$ for H₀, $p = 0.542$ for H₁; $n = 500$). This rules out persistent non-Markovian cyclical trapping in the USoc observation window. The BHPS data introduce a complication, addressed in §6.2.

### §4.5 Regime Stickiness and Escape Dynamics

Overlapping 10-year career-phase windows (5-year step) applied to all 27,280 individuals yield 54,560 windows (mean 2.0 per person). The resulting 7 × 7 transition matrix is strongly diagonal-dominant. The largest self-transitions occur in R2 — Inactive Low (0.97), R1 — Secure High-Employment (0.78), and R4 — Employed Mid (0.66). The two disadvantaged regimes differ sharply: R2 is near-absorbing (97%) while R6 permits moderately higher escape (46%).

The 97% R2 self-transition partly reflects lifecycle inactivity: 86.1% of Inactive Low window-observations come from individuals aged 60 or older. Age-stratified escape rates expose this confound.

Logistic regression on individuals with complete covariate data (*n* = 4,832, pseudo-$R^2$ = 0.479) finds that initial regime is the dominant predictor: individuals starting in R6 have 21 times the escape odds of those starting in R2 (OR = 20.56, 95% CI [10.65, 39.69], $p < 10^{-19}$). Each additional year of age reduces escape odds by 7% (OR = 0.93, $p = 0.006$). Birth cohort effects are large: the 1960s cohort has 24 times the escape odds of the pre-1950 reference (OR = 23.8, $p < 0.001$). Parental NS-SEC is not significant after conditioning on age, cohort, and initial regime. Phase-order shuffle testing ($n = 500$) finds no evidence that life-course ordering of career phases adds topological structure beyond internal sequential structure ($p_\text{H₀} = 0.98$, $p_\text{H₁} = 0.542$).

### §4.6 Sequence Analysis Comparison

Optimal matching with dynamic Hamming distances (Lesnard 2010) on the full sample yields an optimal $k = 2$ solution (silhouette 0.223) that separates predominantly inactive (40.2%) from predominantly employed (59.8%) trajectories. At $k = 7$, OM achieves silhouette = 0.215. The ARI between OM $k = 7$ clusters and GMM $k = 7$ regimes is **0.26**. Regime-level profile correlations range from 0.97 (R1 Secure High-Employment, which both methods identify nearly identically) to 0.02 (Mixed Churn, where OM and TDA draw boundaries differently).

The moderate ARI confirms that broad trajectory types — secure employment, inactivity, churning — are recoverable by either method. TDA's added value is not a different regime typology but the geometric characterisation ($W_2$ diagram comparisons, H₀ merge hierarchy) and formal null-testing infrastructure that sequence analysis cannot provide.

## §5 Within-Regime Anatomy via Mapper

### §5.1 Two Complementary Analytical Lenses

The seven GMM regimes characterise the global landscape of UK career trajectories. Mapper provides two analytical lenses into this landscape. The first lens — using DBSCAN clustering — reveals geometrically dense sub-regions within each regime: trajectories compact enough to cluster at eps = 0.5. The second lens — using agglomerative clustering — provides complete coverage and identifies bridge trajectories that span regime boundaries. The two views are complementary: DBSCAN for robust, conservative sub-regime identification; agglomerative for full boundary analysis. This framing is not a sensitivity analysis but a deliberate two-view design: the two algorithms answer different questions about the interior structure of the trajectory manifold.

### §5.2 DBSCAN View: Dense Sub-Regions

The DBSCAN Mapper configuration (PCA-2D lens, $n_\text{cubes} = 30$, overlap 0.5, eps 0.5) produces **1,060 nodes** and **1,774 edges** forming 223 connected components, covering 50.7% of trajectories (13,827 of 27,280). The remaining 49.3% are DBSCAN noise points — locally sparse relative to their cover element. Mean node purity is 0.999: virtually every node is dominated by a single regime. NMI against GMM labels is 0.434.

[Figure 8: Mapper graph coloured by PC1 income gradient (DBSCAN, eps = 0.5)]

**Within-regime heterogeneity.** Across all seven regimes, 358 of 1,060 nodes deviate more than one standard deviation from their regime's mean on PC1 (the primary income gradient; Table 4). Permutation null tests confirm this is not a distributional artefact: against a regime-shuffle null, the observed 358 sub-regime nodes compare to a null mean of 86.3 (sd = 12.5), range 42–113 ($p < 0.01$); against a within-node-shuffle null, null mean = 9.4 (sd = 5.2), range 0–26 ($p < 0.01$).

### §5.3 Agglomerative View: Complete Coverage and Boundary Trajectories

Agglomerative Mapper (PCA-2D lens, threshold = 1.5) covers 100% of the 27,280 trajectories across 37,772 nodes, with 359 bridge nodes (nodes where no single regime accounts for more than 70% of members). The absence of bridge nodes under DBSCAN — and their appearance under agglomerative clustering — is an algorithm property, not a data property: DBSCAN excludes sparse boundary-region trajectories as noise rather than assigning them to multi-regime nodes. These boundary trajectories are precisely the ones of greatest sociological interest — individuals whose careers straddle two regimes.

Bridge node concentration: the PCA-2D lens produces more bridge nodes (203–449 across threshold levels) than any 1D lens, consistent with its 2D cover creating more boundary intersections. Bridge node counts scale monotonically with the distance threshold, confirming no natural "gap" in the boundary structure: the regime borders are permeable rather than sharp walls.

### §5.4 Outcome Geography

Colouring Mapper nodes by reconstructed trajectory outcomes reveals a multi-layered geography that regime-level statistics conceal.

**Employment rate.** Within R6 (Low-Income Churn), nodes near the R4 (Employed Mid) boundary show mean employment rates near 0.7; nodes near the R2 (Inactive Low) boundary show rates below 0.3 — a within-regime spread of 0.4 that the regime-level mean (0.59) obscures.

**Churning rate.** Mixed Churn (R0) and Employment–Inactive Mix (R3) both show elevated churning but of different character: R3 churning concentrates in employment income-band transitions (38.6% of transitions are within-E income changes) while R5 churning concentrates in inactivity income changes (54.4% within-I transitions). This structural difference — invisible in regime-level means — is visible in the Mapper node colouring and in the transition decomposition below.

**Final income band.** R6 bifurcates: nodes near the R4 boundary trend toward mid-range final income; nodes near R2 remain low. This within-regime divergence on final outcome (churning rate correlation with PC1, $r = 0.12$; final income band with PC1, $r = -0.22$) represents genuine information beyond the dominant embedding gradient, since both outcomes are weakly correlated with PC1 by design.

### §5.5 Churning Decomposition: R3 and R5

Regimes R3 (Employment–Inactive Mix) and R5 (High-Income Inactive) share similar employment rates (~0.41–0.59) and both involve cycling between employment and inactivity. The cross-status (E↔I) transition rate is nearly identical: 36.4% (R3) versus 35.5% (R5). What differentiates them is within-status churning: R3 generates substantial income-band movement while employed (within-E = 38.6%), suggesting volatile earnings; R5 generates movement within inactivity (within-I = 54.4%), consistent with pension income or partner-earnings fluctuations captured by household-level equivalisation. The Mapper geometry makes this distinction spatially explicit: R3 nodes show a wide spread in high-income rate; R5 nodes show concentrated high income but wide variation in inactivity duration.

### §5.6 Sensitivity

The 24-configuration sensitivity analysis (4 lens functions × 3 DBSCAN eps values and 3 agglomerative thresholds) is summarised here; the full grid is in Supplement §S3. Three findings are robust to all configurations. First, within-regime heterogeneity persists: every configuration detects sub-regime structure on both PC1 and L2 norm. Second, regime purity remains above 0.990 across all 24 configurations: the seven regimes occupy geometrically distinct regions. Third, NMI is stable in the range 0.34–0.49. Two findings are algorithm-dependent: the noise rate (0% agglomerative, ~50% DBSCAN) and bridge node count (0–2 under DBSCAN, 86–449 under agglomerative). UMAP-16D replication confirms qualitative robustness to embedding choice (576–828 sub-regime nodes vs. 358 under PCA-20D; NMI 0.40–0.42 vs. 0.43; 81–164 bridge nodes vs. 0).

### §6.1 Stratification by Gender, Parental Class, and Birth Cohort

Pairwise $W_2$ distances between group-specific persistence diagrams $D_q(X)$ test whether subpopulations differ topologically. ### §6.2 BHPS-Era Replication (1991–2008)

The 8,509 BHPS-era trajectories provide a cross-era replication designed to test whether the regime structure and topological findings extend to a different labour market epoch, not to validate the testing methodology (which is developed in the companion paper P01-B).

BIC selects $k = 8$ on BHPS-era embeddings, one regime more than USoc. Core types are comparable: stable high-income employed (stability 0.83), economically inactive low-income (employment 0.0%, stability 0.44), and several churning and mixed regimes — suggesting that the seven-to-eight-component structure reflects genuine career landscape features of the UK labour market across both eras rather than an artefact of USoc-specific sampling.

Null-model tests on BHPS-era trajectories are consistent with the USoc findings. Order-shuffle H₀ is strongly rejected under both total persistence ($p = 0.000$) and $W_2$ diagram distances ($p = 0.000$; mean obs-null $W_2 = 16.17$ vs. null-null $W_2 = 6.36$). Markov-1 is not rejected under total persistence, paralleling USoc. Under Wasserstein testing, Markov-1 H₀ is decisively rejected ($p = 0.000$; mean obs-null $W_2 = 23.18$ vs. null-null $W_2 = 7.05$) — confirming that the test-statistic discrepancy replicates cross-era.

The BHPS data additionally reject Markov-1 H₁ under Wasserstein testing ($p = 0.000$), a result that is borderline in the shorter USoc panel ($p = 0.086$). This difference is consistent with a window-length interpretation: BHPS trajectories average 14.5 years versus 12.9 for USoc, providing greater power to separate loop structure from Markov baselines. Whether it reflects genuinely different cycling dynamics in the 1991–2008 economy or solely the longer observation window is a question for future work. The Wasserstein validation of the regime structure — the central methodological finding — is confirmed in the companion methods paper.

## §7 Discussion

### §7.1 The Geometry of UK Career Inequality

Seven mobility regimes describe UK employment-income trajectories across both the USoc and BHPS eras. They are not arbitrary density modes: their hierarchical connectivity structure, encoded in H₀ persistence diagrams, carries information about the data-generating process that a globally estimated first-order Markov chain cannot reproduce. The regime landscape has internal geometry. The Markov discrepancy localises to second-order memory: Markov-2 reproduces observed diagram configuration ($W_2$ $p = 0.546$), indicating that two-step path dependence — the state two years back — shapes the manifold shape that a single global transition matrix cannot.

The seven regimes vary dramatically in stickiness. Secure High-Employment is the dominant regime by size (27.0%) and among the stickiest (self-transition 0.78). Inactive Low is the stickiest of all (0.97) but this reflects retirement absorptivity more than labour market trapping: working-age escape rates from disadvantaged regimes are 17.8%, over three times the raw overall rate of 5.6%. Low-Income Churn has a lower self-transition (0.46) but is the deeper disadvantage story: individuals in R6 cycle between low-paid employment, unemployment, and inactivity with a 61.1% low-income rate, and their Mapper sub-structure reveals sub-populations at very different distances from the Employed Mid boundary.

Initial regime — not parental class — is the strongest predictor of escape from disadvantage (OR = 20.56 for R6 vs. R2, $p < 10^{-19}$; parental NS-SEC non-significant after controlling for age, cohort, and initial regime). This does not mean that class origin is irrelevant to trajectory type — the NS-SEC stratification results show clear topological differences by parental class — but it does mean that once trajectory type is conditioned on, class origin adds little additional predictive power over escape probability. The implication is that interventions should target the trajectory structure itself, not solely background characteristics.

### §7.2 Within-Regime Boundaries and Policy Cartography

Mapper converts the abstract regime typology into a navigable outcome geography. The within-R6 bifurcation — nodes near Employed Mid with employment rates of 0.7 versus nodes near Inactive Low with rates below 0.3 — identifies two sub-populations within the same regime label who may require qualitatively different support. This is not a claim about mechanisms. The Mapper graph shows *where* in trajectory space outcomes diverge; causal identification of what interventions would be effective requires additional covariates and design. But the cartographic value is real: the sub-population near the R4/R6 boundary is identifiable from trajectory data alone, and targeting them with activation support rather than more intensive structural intervention may be feasible in administrative practice.

The coverage asymmetry under DBSCAN — where churning regimes (R0 covers 6.1% of members, R6 covers 36.6%) are precisely the ones with the lowest coverage, and thus the most underrepresented in DBSCAN-based outcome maps — is a finding that matters for applied policy analysis. Disadvantaged and churning trajectories, the populations of greatest policy interest, are the most geometrically dispersed and therefore the most likely to be DBSCAN noise. The agglomerative complement recovers full coverage at the cost of interpretive complexity; any Mapper application to policy-relevant trajectory data should report both views.

### §7.3 Limitations

**Age and lifecycle confounding.** The near-absorbing R2 (Inactive Low, 97% self-transition) reflects predominantly retired individuals. The age stratification in §4.5 addresses this, but residual confounding between retirement and disability/caring inactivity may remain.

**State space and income thresholds.** The nine-state space uses conventional 60%/120% median thresholds. Alternative threshold choices (50%/150%, population terciles) are not examined; sensitivity here is future work. The restriction to employment status and income omits housing, partnership, and health dimensions that influence both trajectory type and regime stickiness.

**Embedding and embedding sensitivity.** The PCA-20D n-gram embedding determines the geometry of the point cloud. UMAP-16D replication (ARI = 0.33 against PCA at $k = 7$; order-shuffle H₀ $p < 0.001$) confirms qualitative robustness, but fine regime boundaries are embedding-dependent. Alternative sequence representations — optimal matching distance matrices, recurrent neural network embeddings — remain untested.

**Spatial dimension.** The analysis is national and non-spatial. Regional stratification at NUTS2 or Travel-to-Work Area level is a direct extension of the Markov memory ladder and a natural next step.

**NS-SEC missingness.** Parental NS-SEC is missing for 39.1% of the sample in a non-random pattern. All NS-SEC-stratified results carry this caveat.

**Panel attrition.** Low-income workers face higher attrition in household panels (Watson & Wooden 2012); the sample slightly overrepresents stable employment trajectories. Attrition analysis is reported in Supplement §S2.

### §7.4 Conclusion

UK career trajectories have a topological structure that scalar Markov statistics miss. Seven mobility regimes with geometrically distinct H₀ persistence diagrams reveal a landscape of career inequality in which the choice to use Wasserstein diagram comparison — rather than a total-persistence scalar — is decisive for whether first-order Markov dynamics are judged adequate. Within the regime landscape, Mapper analysis of Low-Income Churn exposes a sub-population near the boundary with stable employment that regime-level statistics treat identically to those drifting toward absorbing inactivity. The two populations are not the same; the trajectory space geometry shows where they diverge.

The combined picture — seven regimes, extreme stickiness, mobility boundaries within regimes — describes UK career inequality not as a gradient of outcomes but as a topographic landscape with barriers, boundaries, and absorbing regions. The Markov memory ladder, replicated across two survey eras spanning 1991–2023, localises the structural complexity to two-step path dependence. The policy implication is that changing individual transition probabilities at a single step will not reshape the regime landscape; what matters is the sub-region of trajectory space from which an individual begins, and the distance between that sub-region and the boundary with more stable regimes.
