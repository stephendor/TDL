## Summary of Contribution

Paper 1 establishes that UK employment trajectory space exhibits topological complexity exceeding first-order Markov expectations, with persistent H0 and H1 features across Vietoris-Rips filtrations. That analysis uses a single filtration parameter (Rips distance), which treats all individuals identically regardless of income level. This paper introduces a **bifiltration** — simultaneous filtration by pairwise distance and income threshold — that decomposes the trajectory topology into income-stratified layers. The central question: do topological features cluster distinctively at low-income thresholds, providing formal evidence for poverty traps as topological objects?

Multi-parameter persistent homology does not admit a complete barcode decomposition analogous to single-parameter PH (Carlsson & Zomorodian, 2009). We use the `multipers` library's signed barcode decomposition (NeurIPS 2023) and Hilbert function / rank invariant to characterise the bifiltration. Permutation null tests (shuffling income labels) assess whether the income-stratified topology differs from what distance alone would predict.

## 2. Introduction (~1,500 words)

### 2.1 Motivation: The Limitations of Single-Parameter Topology

- P01's VR PH captures global shape of trajectory space — clusters, loops, connectivity
- But the Rips filtration treats all pairwise distances equally regardless of economic outcome
- A trajectory cluster in a low-income region of the space has the same topological weight as one in a high-income region
- Poverty trap theory (Azariadis & Stachurski, 2005; Barrett & Carter, 2013) predicts structurally distinct *low-income* configurations — absorbing states, threshold effects, bifurcation
- Single-parameter PH cannot test this prediction because it lacks an income axis
- Multi-parameter PH (Carlsson & Zomorodian, 2009) provides exactly the mathematical framework to introduce income as a second filtration parameter

### 2.2 Multi-Parameter Persistence: From Theory to Computation

- Multi-parameter PH has been theoretically developed since Carlsson & Zomorodian (2009), but computationally intractable for applied data until recently
- The `multipers` library (Loiseaux et al., NeurIPS 2023, JOSS 2024) makes bifiltration computation practical via signed barcode decomposition and autodiff
- Connects to the broader "applied multi-parameter persistence" programme: Lesnick & Wright (2015), RIVET (Lesnick & Wright, 2022), Vipond (2020)
- This paper is among the first applications to social science panel data

### 2.3 Research Questions

1. Does the bifiltration (distance × income) reveal topological features absent from single-parameter Rips PH?
2. Do persistent features at low-income thresholds correspond to poverty trap regimes identified by P01's GMM typology?
3. Is income-stratified topology robust to permutation of income labels?
4. Which multi-parameter invariant is most informative for this data? *[Resolved: H1 signed measure with income-quartile decomposition — see §4.3]*

### 2.4 Paper Roadmap

Literature → Data and Bifiltration Construction → Multi-Parameter PH Methods → Results → Discussion

## 3. Literature Review (~1,800 words)

### 3.1 Poverty Traps and Threshold Effects

- Poverty trap theory: multiple equilibria, self-reinforcing mechanisms (Azariadis & Stachurski, 2005)
- Structural vs stochastic traps (Barrett & Carter, 2013)
- Income thresholds and absorbing states in Markov models of mobility (Cappellari & Jenkins, 2004)
- UK context: persistent low-pay, no-pay cycling (Shildrick et al., 2012)
- **Gap:** Poverty trap identification in trajectory data lacks topological formalisation

### 3.2 Single-Parameter Persistent Homology for Social Science

- P01 contribution: VR PH on UK employment trajectories, Markov memory ladder null testing
- Related work: trajectory topology in labour economics (novel — P01 is foundational)
- Limitations: single-parameter PH is outcome-agnostic — income is not part of the filtration
- Mapper (P02, Singh et al., 2007) provides complementary *graph-based* structure but not persistence

### 3.3 Multi-Parameter Persistent Homology

- **Theoretical foundations:** Carlsson & Zomorodian (2009) — no complete barcode decomposition exists for multi-parameter PH
- **The rank invariant:** complete discrete invariant for 2-parameter PH (Carlsson & Zomorodian, 2009)
- **Fibred barcode / signed barcode:** Botnan, Oppermann, Oudot (2022); Loiseaux et al. (NeurIPS 2023) — recent breakthrough making computation practical
- **Hilbert function:** graded Betti numbers across bifiltration grid — interpretable as "feature count at each (distance, income) pair"
- **RIVET:** interactive 2-parameter visualisation tool (Lesnick & Wright, 2022)
- **Euler surfaces:** from multipers — summary statistic for bifiltration
- **Landscape-based invariants:** Vipond (2020) multi-parameter persistence landscapes

### 3.4 Computational Tools

- `multipers` library: scikit-style API, gudhi backend, PyTorch autodiff (Loiseaux et al., JOSS 2024)
- Supports: signed barcodes, Hilbert functions, rank invariants, Euler surfaces, sliced Wasserstein
- Integrates with gudhi simplex trees
- Alternative: RIVET (C++, visualisation-focused) — we use `multipers` for its statistical testing capabilities

## 4. Data and Methods (~2,500 words)

### 4.1 Data: BHPS/USoc Panel Trajectories

- **Source:** British Household Panel Survey (1991–2008) and Understanding Society (2009–2023)
- **Sample:** Same as P01 — individuals with ≥5 waves of employment state observations
- **Embedding:** n-gram PCA (from P01 pipeline) — each individual is a point in ~20D space
- **Income variable:** Mean equivalised household income across observed waves (per-person scalar)
  - Source: USoc derived variable `fihhmnnet1_dv` (net household income, monthly)
  - BHPS equivalent: `fihhmn` (household income, monthly, net)
  - Equivalisation: Modified OECD scale
  - Deflated to 2019 prices using CPI

### 4.2 Bifiltration Construction

The key methodological step. We construct a two-parameter filtration:

**Parameter 1 — Rips distance (ε):** Standard Vietoris-Rips filtration on the PCA embedding. An edge between individuals $i$ and $j$ appears when $\|x_i - x_j\| \leq \varepsilon$. This captures geometric proximity in trajectory space.

**Parameter 2 — Income threshold (τ):** A vertex $i$ enters the complex when its mean income $y_i \leq \tau$. This restricts the complex to individuals below a given income level, building up from the poorest trajectories.

Formally, let $X = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ be the PCA embedding and $\{y_1, \ldots, y_n\}$ the mean incomes. The bifiltration $\{K_{\varepsilon, \tau}\}$ is:

$$K_{\varepsilon, \tau} = \text{VR}_\varepsilon(\{x_i \mid y_i \leq \tau\})$$

where $\text{VR}_\varepsilon$ is the Vietoris-Rips complex at scale $\varepsilon$.

**Income discretisation:** We discretise $\tau$ using a **quantile grid**: 20 thresholds at uniform quantiles (ventiles) of the landmark income distribution, ensuring approximately equal vertex counts per grid step. This is the primary analysis.

*Empirical decision (exploratory computation, April 2026):*
- Quantile vs fixed (uniform-spacing) grids were compared on 2,000 maxmin landmarks from the P01 checkpoint.
- Both grids produce the same qualitative finding: H1 signed measure mass concentrates in upper income quartiles (Q3/Q4 carry ~4× the mass of Q1).
- Quantile grid has superior resolution stability: H1 low-income fraction converges to ~0.33 by 10 grid steps (range [0.33, 0.44]) vs fixed grid (range [0.26, 0.51]).
- Quantile grid produces balanced H0 stratification (51/49) vs fixed grid's high-income bias (45/55).
- **Decision:** Quantile grid as primary; fixed grid reported as sensitivity check in appendix.
- Results: `results/p04_exploration/discretisation_exploration.json`

**Landmarking:** For computational tractability, subsample $n$ landmarks from the full embedding:
- Development: $n = 2{,}000$ landmarks (local, minutes)
- Full scale: $n = 5{,}000$ landmarks (local, hours) or $n = 8{,}000+$ (cloud)
- Landmarking via maxmin subsampling to preserve geometric coverage

### 4.3 Multi-Parameter Persistence Computation

Using `multipers` library:

1. **Signed measure** (primary invariant): The signed measure $\mu_k$ for homology degree $k$ is a discrete measure on the $(ε, τ)$ plane that encodes where topological features are born and die in the bifiltration. We evaluate it on the quantile grid defined in §4.2. The resulting heatmap reveals income-stratified concentration of topological features.

   *Empirical decision (April 2026):* Exploratory computation on 2,000 landmarks showed H1 signed measure mass has a pronounced income gradient: quartile masses Q1≈20k, Q2≈35k, Q3≈79k, Q4≈78k. The H1 low-income fraction (~0.33) is well below the 0.50 expected under income-exchangeability, providing the key test statistic for permutation testing.

2. **Income-quartile decomposition** of signed measure mass: We partition the $τ$ axis into income quartiles and report total absolute signed measure mass per quartile. This gives a four-number summary interpretable by JASA's applied audience: "how much topological complexity exists at each income level?"

3. **Signed measure heatmap** across the full $(ε, τ)$ grid: The primary visualisation. Signed weight at each grid cell shows where H1 cycles appear (positive) and disappear (negative) in the bifiltration.

4. **Euler surface** $\chi(\varepsilon, \tau) = \sum_k (-1)^k \dim H_k(K_{\varepsilon, \tau})$: Reported as a robustness check. Exploratory computation confirmed it adds no interpretive value beyond H1 (all strongly negative, same income gradient).

The signed measure is the primary invariant for the main text. Two theoretically richer invariants — the **Hilbert function** and the **rank invariant** — are developed in parallel and reported in Appendix B. These invariants carry strictly more information than the signed measure but require more algebraic machinery to interpret. We include them to preserve the analytical depth for readers with a TDA background, and because they may prove essential for the cross-national comparison in P05 or for a standalone methodological treatment at a different venue.

**Computational pipeline:**

```
P01 embedding (27,280 × 20D) → maxmin landmarks (2,000–8,000)
                               → attach mean incomes
                               → construct gudhi SimplexTree with bifiltration values
                               → multipers.signed_barcode()
                               → multipers.hilbert_function()
                               → multipers.rank_invariant()
```

### 4.4 Null Testing

**Null hypothesis:** Income-stratified topology is fully explained by the distance structure — i.e., shuffling income labels does not change the multi-parameter invariants.

**Permutation procedure:**
1. Compute observed multi-parameter invariant $I_\text{obs}$ (e.g., Hilbert function integrated over low-income region)
2. For $b = 1, \ldots, B$ permutations:
   a. Shuffle income labels across landmarks (break income-trajectory association)
   b. Recompute bifiltration with shuffled incomes
   c. Compute $I_b$
3. p-value: $\hat{p} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B \mathbb{1}[I_b \geq I_\text{obs}]$

**Test statistic (decided after exploratory computation):**
- **Primary:** H1 low-income fraction = (H1 signed measure mass below median income) / (total H1 signed measure mass). Observed value ≈ 0.33 on quantile grid. Under the null (income exchangeable with distance), this should average ~0.50.
- **Secondary:** Quartile mass ratio Q1/Q3 (observed ≈ 20k/79k ≈ 0.26). Under the null, should be close to 1.0 if income structure is irrelevant.

**Permutation count:** $B = 999$ (standard for p < 0.01 detection)

### 4.5 Comparison to P01 Single-Parameter Baseline

- Run standard VR PH on same landmarks (ignoring income) — this is the P01 baseline
- Compare: does bifiltration reveal features masked by single-parameter PH?
- Specifically: features that appear only when restricting to low-income individuals ($τ \leq Q_1$)
- If low-income subcomplex has H1 features absent from full complex, this is direct evidence of income-stratified topology

## 5. Results (~2,000 words)

### 5.1 Bifiltration Structure

**Preliminary findings (2,000 landmarks, exploratory computation):**

- Bifiltration on 27,280 × 20D embedding → 2,000 maxmin landmarks → 215,958 simplices (Rips radius 11.13)
- Income score range [0, 2]; mean = 1.02, median = 1.00 (balanced around midpoint)
- H0 signed measure: 229 support points (quantile grid), roughly balanced across income (low_frac = 0.51)
- H1 signed measure: 218 support points, strongly income-stratified (low_frac = 0.33)

**Key finding:** H0 (connected components) is income-symmetric — clusters form at all income levels. But H1 (loops/cycles) concentrates at high income — topological complexity is income-stratified.

### 5.2 Income-Stratified H1 Topology

- H1 quartile mass decomposition (both grids agree): Q1 ≈ 20k, Q2 ≈ 35k, Q3 ≈ 79k, Q4 ≈ 78k
- Topological cycles are ~4× more prevalent at Q3/Q4 than at Q1
- Heatmap [Figure 2]: H1 signed measure weight concentrated in the (high-ε, high-τ) quadrant — loops form among high-income individuals at moderate-to-large Rips scales
- **Interpretation:** Mid-to-high income trajectory space has diverse connectivity (employment-hours combinations, multiple career paths coexisting) that creates cycles. Low-income trajectory space is more "linear" — fewer alternative pathways.
- This is the poverty trap signal reframed: poverty traps are structurally *simpler* — low-dimensional, with fewer escape routes creating topological complexity

- [Figure 3] Fixed grid heatmap: signal concentrates in upper portion, with sharp contrast at transition region (τ ≈ 1.0)
- Negative weights (blue) visible at low-τ, moderate-ε: features that die when income threshold is still low

## 6. Discussion (~1,500 words)

### 6.1 What Multi-Parameter PH Adds Beyond Single-Parameter

- Single-parameter PH: "trajectory space has non-trivial topology"
- Multi-parameter PH: "non-trivial topology concentrates at low-income thresholds"
- The bifiltration provides a formal income-aware decomposition that connects topology to poverty trap theory
- This is not possible with post-hoc income stratification of single-parameter PH (which changes the point cloud)

### 6.2 Poverty Traps as Topological Objects

- If confirmed: persistent H1 features at low τ represent closed trajectory cycles — individuals cycling between low-income states without escape
- These cycles are topological poverty traps: geometrically persistent structures in income-stratified trajectory space
- Connects to Barrett & Carter's structural poverty trap definition via formal mathematical language
- Policy implication: breaking H1 cycles requires structural intervention at specific income thresholds

### 6.3 Methodological Contribution

- Among first applications of `multipers` bifiltration to social science data
- Demonstrates viability of multi-parameter PH for panel survey data at moderate scale
- Computational guidance for applied researchers: landmark count, income discretisation, invariant choice

### 6.4 Limitations

- Income discretisation introduces approximation (continuous τ would be ideal but computationally prohibitive)
- Landmark subsampling may miss rare trajectory types
- Cross-sectional income vs permanent income: mean trajectory income is a proxy
- 2-parameter only — could extend to 3+ parameters (income × education × geography) but computational cost scales sharply
- Dependence on P01 embedding quality

### 6.5 Future Directions

- P05 cross-national: apply bifiltration to SOEP/PSID for comparative welfare-state topology
- Higher-parameter filtrations with `multipers` 3D support

## Appendix B: Hilbert Function and Rank Invariant Analysis

*Status: ongoing. Included as appendix for the JASA submission; may be developed into a standalone methodological companion piece or blog post for a TDA audience.*

### B.1 Hilbert Function

The Hilbert function $\mathcal{H}_k(\varepsilon, \tau) = \dim H_k(K_{\varepsilon, \tau})$ gives the number of $k$-dimensional topological features alive at each grid point in the bifiltration. Unlike the signed measure — which records *changes* in topology (births and deaths) — the Hilbert function records the *state* of topology at each $(\varepsilon, \tau)$ pair.

For an applied audience the Hilbert function answers a direct question: "at Rips scale $\varepsilon$ and income threshold $\tau$, how many connected components / loops / voids exist?" A heatmap of $\mathcal{H}_1$ across the grid would show the regions of $(\varepsilon, \tau)$ space where loop structures are most prevalent, complementing the signed measure's differential view.

**Computation:** `multipers` provides `mp.hilbert_function(st, degree=k, ...)` which evaluates the Hilbert function on a grid. Memory and time scale with grid resolution squared, so coarser grids (10 × 10) are advisable for initial exploration.

**Planned analyses:**
- $\mathcal{H}_1$ heatmap at 10 × 10 and 20 × 20 resolution on 2,000 landmarks
- Comparison of observed heatmap vs mean of 99 income-permuted heatmaps (pixelwise z-scores)
- Identification of $(\varepsilon, \tau)$ regions where $\mathcal{H}_1$ is significantly elevated or depressed relative to the null

### B.2 Rank Invariant

The rank invariant $\rho_k(a, b) = \text{rank}(H_k(K_a) \to H_k(K_b))$ for $a \leq b$ in the bifiltration order is the complete discrete invariant for 2-parameter PH (Carlsson & Zomorodian, 2009). It captures not just how many features exist at each point, but which features *persist* across parameter ranges — the multi-parameter analogue of persistence.

For poverty trap detection, the rank invariant answers: "how many H1 features born in the low-income, small-scale region survive into the high-income, large-scale region?" A high rank along the $(\text{low-}\tau, \text{low-}\varepsilon) \to (\text{high-}\tau, \text{high-}\varepsilon)$ diagonal would indicate features that persist across the full income range — global structures. A rank that drops sharply as $\tau$ increases would indicate features confined to low-income trajectory space.

**Computation:** The rank invariant is more expensive than the signed measure — $O(n^2)$ in grid resolution per pair of points. `multipers` supports it via `mp.rank_invariant()` but at 20 × 20 resolution the output is a 400 × 400 matrix. We plan to compute it at reduced resolution (10 × 10) and summarise via the rank along specific diagonal slices.

**Planned analyses:**
- Rank along the main diagonal $(\varepsilon_i, \tau_i) \to (\varepsilon_j, \tau_j)$ for $i < j$
- "Persistence of income-specific features": rank from $(\varepsilon, \tau_{\text{low}})$ to $(\varepsilon, \tau_{\text{high}})$ at fixed $\varepsilon$ — how many features survive income increases?
- Comparison to single-parameter persistence barcode from P01

### B.3 Venue Considerations for Extended Treatment

The Hilbert function and rank invariant analyses are methodologically substantial and may warrant their own treatment beyond this paper's appendix:

- **TDA-focused journal** (e.g., *Journal of Applied and Computational Topology*): Full development of all three invariants (signed measure, Hilbert function, rank invariant) on the same bifiltration, with a comparative assessment of statistical power and interpretability for social science data.
