## Abstract

We apply the Mapper algorithm (Singh, Mémoli, & Carlsson 2007) to 27,280 employment-income trajectories from Understanding Society (2009–2023), embedded in PCA-20D space. The baseline Mapper graph constructed with DBSCAN clustering (1,060 nodes, 1,774 edges, 223 connected components) reproduces the seven Gaussian mixture model (GMM) regimes with near-perfect node purity (0.999) and moderate normalised mutual information (NMI = 0.434). Within regimes, Mapper nodes vary substantially on the PC1 income gradient: Low-Income Churn fragments into 80 nodes spanning a 4-unit PC1 range (node-mean std = 1.23), and 32 of these deviate by more than one standard deviation from the regime mean. Across all seven regimes, 358 of 1,060 nodes qualify as sub-regime outliers. A systematic sensitivity analysis across 24 configurations — varying the clustering algorithm (DBSCAN vs. agglomerative), DBSCAN neighbourhood radius, agglomerative distance threshold, and lens function (PCA-2D, L2 norm, sum, kernel density) — reveals that the 49% noise rate observed under DBSCAN is algorithm-specific: agglomerative clustering achieves 100% coverage while identifying 203–449 bridge nodes that span regime boundaries, invisible under DBSCAN. Within-regime heterogeneity is robust across all configurations, confirming it as a geometric property of the trajectory manifold rather than a parameter artefact.

**Keywords:** Mapper algorithm, topological data analysis, trajectory analysis, within-regime heterogeneity, employment trajectories, KeplerMapper, sensitivity analysis

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Limitation of Global Summaries

Recent applications of topological data analysis to longitudinal social data have demonstrated that persistent homology (PH) can detect structural features of trajectory space that conventional sequence analysis misses. In a companion paper (Author, 2026a), we applied Vietoris–Rips persistent homology to the same trajectory embedding analysed here, finding that temporal ordering creates topological structure (order-shuffle H₀ *p* < 0.005), that Wasserstein diagram-level tests reject the first-order Markov null even when scalar total-persistence tests do not (*p* = 0.002 vs. *p* = 1.000), and that seven mobility regimes exhibit extreme stickiness (5.6% escape rate from disadvantaged regimes across overlapping career-phase windows).

These findings establish the global topology of the trajectory manifold. But global topology is, by construction, a summary. The persistence diagram records that trajectory clusters exist and how they merge as the filtration radius increases. It does not record what happens *inside* those clusters — whether a regime labelled "Low-Income Churn" is internally homogeneous or contains sub-populations with markedly different prospects. The seven-component GMM that defines the regime typology has the same limitation: it assigns each trajectory a single label, collapsing any within-regime variation into a regime-level mean.

The compression has practical consequences. If the Low-Income Churn regime contains both a sub-region where 25% of trajectories eventually escape to stable employment and another where virtually none do, the regime-level escape rate of 5.6% is a misleading average. Interventions targeting "Low-Income Churn" as a homogeneous population will misallocate resources, treating individuals near the boundary with stable employment identically to those deep in the near-absorbing core.

### 1.2 Mapper as Interior Explorer

The Mapper algorithm (Singh, Mémoli & Carlsson 2007) works at a different resolution. Where persistent homology computes a global topological invariant (the persistence diagram), Mapper constructs a *graph-based simplicial summary* that preserves local geometric structure. The algorithm projects the point cloud through a *lens function* to a lower-dimensional space, covers the lens image with overlapping intervals, clusters the pre-image of each interval, and records overlaps between clusters as edges. The result is a graph in which nodes are geometrically coherent groups of data points and edges connect groups that share members.

The graph can be *coloured* by any variable defined on the data points. Computing the mean of an outcome variable within each node transforms the abstract graph into a map on which outcomes are geographically readable. Regions where escape probability is high become visually and statistically distinguishable from regions where it is low, even when both carry the same GMM label. We call this a *causal geography* of trajectory space.

Mapper has been applied in biomedicine (Lum et al. 2013; Rizvi et al. 2017; Li et al. 2015), political science (Lum et al. 2013), and sports analytics (Alagappan 2015), but has seen little uptake in life-course sociology, where the question is not merely "how many types exist?" but "where within each type do outcomes diverge?"

### 1.3 Contribution

We make three contributions. First, we apply Mapper to the PCA-20D trajectory embedding from Author (2026a), showing that it reproduces the seven GMM regimes at the aggregate level while fragmenting the churning regimes into sub-regions with detectably different positions along the income gradient. Second, we conduct a systematic sensitivity analysis across 24 configurations varying the clustering algorithm, its parameters, and the lens function, establishing that within-regime heterogeneity is robust to all tested choices. Third, we show that algorithm choice qualitatively changes two findings: the noise rate (49% under DBSCAN, 0% under agglomerative clustering) and the existence of bridge nodes spanning regime boundaries (zero under DBSCAN, hundreds under agglomerative), demonstrating that results attributed to data geometry can reflect algorithm geometry instead.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Mapper: Theory and Applications

The Mapper algorithm was introduced by Singh, Mémoli, and Carlsson (2007) as a method for producing simplified, graph-based summaries of high-dimensional point clouds. Mathematically, Mapper approximates the Reeb graph of a function defined on a topological space — a quotient space that collapses each connected component of each level set to a point. For finite point clouds, the approximation depends on a cover of the range of a lens function, with partial clustering replacing connected components.

The algorithm requires three choices: (1) a *lens function* (or *projection*) $f: X \to \mathbb{R}^k$ that maps the data to a low-dimensional space; (2) a *cover* of $f(X)$ consisting of overlapping intervals (parameterised by the number of intervals and the overlap fraction); and (3) a *clustering algorithm* applied to the pre-image of each cover element. Common lens functions include PCA projections, density estimators, and eccentricity measures. The overlap fraction controls the connectivity of the resulting graph: higher overlap produces more edges and a more connected graph; lower overlap produces a sparser graph with more isolated components.

KeplerMapper (van Veen & Saul 2019) provides the standard Python implementation, with built-in support for multiple projections, DBSCAN and agglomerative clustering, and interactive HTML visualisation. The software has been used in genomics (Rizvi et al. 2017), where Mapper revealed branching differentiation trajectories in single-cell RNA-seq data; in political science (Lum et al. 2013), where it identified subgroups within racial profiling data invisible to standard clustering; and in materials science, where it provided visual summaries of high-dimensional phase spaces.

### 2.2 Mapper and Persistent Homology: Complementary Views

Persistent homology and Mapper address different questions about the same point cloud. PH computes global topological invariants — the number and persistence of connected components, loops, and voids — that characterise the multi-scale shape of the entire space. These invariants are coordinate-free, mathematically stable, and amenable to formal hypothesis testing via null-model batteries (Robinson & Turner 2017; Author, 2026a).

Mapper, by contrast, produces a coordinate-dependent graph that preserves local geometric structure at the cost of global topological guarantees. The Mapper graph is not a topological invariant: it depends on the choice of lens function, cover parameters, and clustering algorithm. What it provides instead is *navigability* — the ability to inspect individual regions of the data space, colour them by outcome variables, and identify sub-populations that global summaries compress away.

PH answers: "Does the trajectory space have significant topological structure, and under which null models?" Mapper answers: "Given that structure exists, what does it look like locally, and how do outcomes vary across it?" This paper operationalises the second question.

### 2.3 Within-Regime Heterogeneity in Life-Course Research

The recognition that typological labels can mask substantial within-type variation is not new to life-course research. Halpin (2010) noted that sequence-analysis clusters are often internally heterogeneous, with silhouette scores rarely exceeding 0.3. Studer (2013) proposed discrepancy analysis to test whether group differences in sequence data are statistically significant, acknowledging that cluster membership is an imperfect proxy for trajectory similarity.

The specific problem of within-regime heterogeneity in mobility outcomes has received less attention. Jenkins (2011) documented extensive income churning in the UK, noting that individuals with similar annual income trajectories can have very different long-run outcomes depending on the timing and sequencing of income shocks. Vandecasteele (2010) distinguished between "transient" and "recurrent" poverty trajectories that would typically be assigned to the same cluster. These observations motivate a method that can detect sub-regime structure without imposing additional parametric assumptions — precisely what Mapper provides.

### 2.4 Comparison with Optimal Matching

Optimal matching (OM) and its variants (Lesnard 2010; Studer & Ritschard 2016) produce pairwise distance matrices that can be clustered into trajectory types. This approach has been productive but has limitations for within-type analysis. OM distances summarise the total dissimilarity between two sequences without localising which aspects differ. Cutting the OM dendrogram at a given height produces flat clusters with no internal structure. Mapper produces a graph with internal topology: branches, hubs, and peripheries that correspond to distinct geometric sub-regions of each cluster.

In our companion paper (Author, 2026a), the adjusted Rand index between OM (*k* = 7) and GMM (*k* = 7) clusters was 0.26, indicating overlapping but non-identical boundaries. The moderate concordance suggests that broad trajectory types (secure employment, inactivity, churning) are recoverable by either method, while finer distinctions depend on the analytic approach. Mapper operates downstream of both methods: it takes the embedding as input and reveals structure within whatever typology is imposed.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 Data Source and Trajectory Construction

We reuse the trajectory dataset from our companion paper (Author, 2026a). Briefly: from Understanding Society (USoc, waves 1–14, 2009–2023), we reconstruct 27,280 individual trajectories with at least 10 consecutive years of valid employment-income observations. Each person-year is assigned to one of nine states crossing employment status (employed, unemployed, inactive) with income band (low, mid, high), where low is defined as below 60% of the contemporary equivalised household income median, mid as 60–120%, and high as above 120%.

Mean trajectory length is 12.9 years (median 13, range 10–14). The sample is 56% female; birth cohorts span the 1930s through the 1990s. Forward-fill imputation affected 4.9% of person-years. Full data construction details, including BHPS cross-wave linkage and equivalisation procedures, are reported in Author (2026a, §3.1).

### 3.2 Embedding

Trajectories are embedded in continuous space via n-gram frequency vectors. For each trajectory, we compute unigram (9 dimensions: relative frequency of each state) and bigram (81 dimensions: relative frequency of each consecutive state pair) frequencies, yielding a 90-dimensional term-frequency vector. After standardisation, we project to 20 dimensions via PCA, retaining the same frozen PCA loadings used in Author (2026a). The resulting point cloud of 27,280 points in $\mathbb{R}^{20}$ serves as input to both persistent homology (reported in the companion paper) and Mapper (reported here).

### 3.3 Mapper Construction

We construct Mapper graphs using KeplerMapper 2.1 (van Veen & Saul 2019). The algorithm involves three configurable steps — lens function, cover, and clustering — each of which we vary systematically in our sensitivity analysis (§3.5).

**Lens function.** The lens function projects the PCA-20D embedding to a lower-dimensional space that defines the cover. Our primary lens projects onto the first two principal components (PCA-2D), preserving the dominant axes of variation — the employment-inactivity and income gradients. We additionally evaluate three alternative lenses: L2 norm (distance from the embedding centroid), sum projection, and kernel density estimation (KDE with bandwidth 1.0). These vary in dimensionality (1D vs. 2D) and in what aspect of the data they emphasise: PCA-2D tracks the two dominant gradients, L2 norm tracks trajectory extremity, sum captures aggregate n-gram magnitude, and KDE density emphasises local crowding.

**Cover.** The lens image is covered by a grid of overlapping hypercubes. Following parameter tuning in the companion analysis (§3.5), we set n_cubes = 30 and overlap fraction = 0.5 as the baseline. Higher n_cubes produce finer-grained graphs; higher overlap produces greater connectivity.

**Clustering.** We evaluate two clustering algorithms within each cover element:

1. *DBSCAN* (Ester et al. 1996) with eps $\in \{0.2, 0.3, 0.5\}$ and min_samples = 5. DBSCAN does not require pre-specifying the number of clusters and naturally handles noise points (which are excluded from Mapper nodes). Varying eps controls the neighbourhood radius: smaller eps produces tighter clusters with more noise; larger eps produces broader clusters with less noise.

2. *Agglomerative clustering* with distance threshold $\in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ and no pre-specified cluster count. Unlike DBSCAN, agglomerative clustering assigns every point to a cluster — there are no noise points, and coverage is necessarily 100%. Varying the distance threshold controls the granularity: lower thresholds produce more, smaller clusters.

**Scaling.** Lens values are MinMax-scaled before cover construction, ensuring that the cover grid spans the data range in each lens dimension.

### 3.4 Node Colouring and Outcome Variables

For each Mapper node, we compute summary statistics (mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum, count) for two continuous outcome variables:

1. **PC1 (income gradient proxy).** The first principal component of the PCA-20D embedding, which correlates with the employment-inactivity and income gradients. Higher PC1 values correspond to positions nearer the inactivity-low territory; lower values correspond to employed-high territory.

2. **L2 norm (trajectory extremity).** The Euclidean distance from the embedding centroid. Higher L2 norm indicates more atypical career paths.

Additionally, for each node we compute the **regime distribution**: the fraction of member trajectories belonging to each of the seven GMM regimes.

### 3.5 Sensitivity Analysis Design

We conduct a systematic sensitivity analysis across 24 configurations formed by the full cross of:

- **Lens function:** PCA-2D, L2 norm, sum, KDE density (4 levels)
- **DBSCAN eps:** 0.2, 0.3, 0.5 (3 levels → 12 DBSCAN configurations)
- **Agglomerative threshold:** 1.0, 1.5, 2.0 (3 levels → 12 agglomerative configurations)

All configurations use the same cover parameters (n_cubes = 30, overlap = 0.5). For each configuration, we record: (a) graph topology (nodes, edges, components, coverage); (b) regime validation (NMI, mean node purity, bridge node count); and (c) sub-regime structure (number of nodes with |z| > 1.0 on PC1 and L2 norm relative to regime means).

### 3.6 Validation Against GMM Regimes

We validate the Mapper decomposition against the seven GMM regimes using three metrics:

**Normalised mutual information (NMI).** Each trajectory that appears in at least one Mapper node is assigned the label of its node (first-assignment for multiply-assigned points). NMI between this labelling and the GMM regime labels measures correspondence. NMI = 1 indicates perfect agreement; NMI = 0 indicates independence.

**Node purity.** For each node, the fraction of members belonging to the dominant regime. High mean purity indicates that Mapper nodes are internally regime-homogeneous.

**Bridge nodes.** Nodes where no single regime accounts for more than 70% of members. Bridge nodes span regime boundaries and may identify transitional populations.

### 3.7 Within-Regime Heterogeneity Detection

To detect within-regime sub-structure, we apply the following procedure for each of the seven GMM regimes:

1. Identify all Mapper nodes where the majority of members belong to the focal regime.
2. Compute the mean of the outcome variable (PC1 or L2 norm) for each such node.
3. Compute the regime-level mean and standard deviation of the outcome variable across all trajectories assigned to that regime in the full dataset.
4. For each majority-regime node, compute a z-score for the node-level mean relative to the trajectory-level regime mean and standard deviation, and flag nodes where |z| > 1.0.

Flagged nodes are the sub-regions where outcomes detectably differ from the regime average.

#### 4.5.1 Findings Robust to Algorithm Choice

**Within-regime heterogeneity persists across all 24 configurations.** Every DBSCAN and agglomerative configuration detects sub-regime structure on both PC1 and L2 norm. The number of sub-regime nodes varies with resolution (more nodes → more sub-regime nodes), but the qualitative pattern — R6 and R5 most heterogeneous, R3 least — is invariant. This confirms that within-regime heterogeneity is a geometric property of the trajectory manifold, not an artefact of a particular parameter choice.

**Coverage is determined by the clustering algorithm, not the cover.** Within each algorithm class, coverage is effectively constant across lens functions and parameter values: ~40% at DBSCAN eps = 0.2, ~44% at eps = 0.3, ~51% at eps = 0.5, and 100% for all agglomerative configurations. The cover parameters (n_cubes, overlap) control graph resolution; the clustering algorithm controls what fraction of the sample enters the graph at all.

**Purity remains high (> 0.99) across all configurations.** Even agglomerative clustering, which produces far larger graphs (13,620–44,396 nodes), maintains purity above 0.990. The seven GMM regimes correspond to geometrically distinct regions regardless of how points are assigned to clusters within cover elements.

**NMI is stable in a narrow range (0.34–0.49).** All 24 configurations produce NMI between 0.339 and 0.488. The highest NMI values occur with 1D lenses (L2 norm: 0.485, sum: 0.488 at DBSCAN eps = 0.5), which produce fewer, larger nodes that aggregate more closely to the GMM labelling.

#### 4.5.2 Findings That Depend on Algorithm Choice

**The noise rate is a DBSCAN property, not a data property.** DBSCAN excludes 40–60% of trajectories as noise, concentrated in the churning regimes (Mixed Churn: 94% noise, Low-Income Churn: 63% noise). Agglomerative clustering eliminates this entirely: every trajectory is assigned to a node. The "churning trajectories are too geometrically dispersed to cluster" narrative holds only for DBSCAN at the tested eps values. Under hierarchical merging, the same trajectories form small but valid clusters.

**Bridge nodes are algorithm-dependent.** Under DBSCAN, zero or very few bridge nodes exist (0–2 across all 12 DBSCAN configurations). Under agglomerative clustering, 86–449 bridge nodes appear, scaling with the distance threshold and lens dimensionality. The PCA-2D lens produces the most bridge nodes (203–449), consistent with its 2D cover creating more boundary interactions. The regime boundaries are not as geometrically sharp as the DBSCAN results suggest — the sharpness reflects DBSCAN's exclusion of boundary-region points as noise rather than the absence of boundary-spanning trajectories.

### 4.6 Lens Function Effects

The four lens functions produce structurally similar Mapper graphs with interpretable differences (Figure 7). PCA-2D, the only 2D lens, produces the most nodes and edges (1,060 nodes, 1,774 edges at DBSCAN eps = 0.5) because its 2D cover grid has more cells than any 1D cover. The 1D lenses (L2 norm, sum, density) produce sparser graphs (494–586 nodes, 265–377 edges) but with comparable coverage.

L2 norm and sum lenses produce higher NMI (0.485, 0.488) than PCA-2D (0.434) or density (0.416), suggesting that these 1D projections align more closely with the GMM's classification boundaries. The density lens, despite capturing local crowding, does not improve regime correspondence — possibly because the GMM already captures density modes.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What Mapper Adds to Global Persistent Homology

The companion paper (Author, 2026a) established that UK trajectory space has significant topological structure: temporal ordering creates cluster separation (order-shuffle H₀ *p* < 0.005), and Wasserstein diagram-level distances reject the first-order Markov null (*p* = 0.002). The Mapper analysis shifts from global shape to local geography.

The seven GMM regimes are geometrically distinct — purity exceeds 0.99 across all 24 tested configurations. But within these pure-regime regions, Mapper fragments each regime into multiple nodes that vary substantially on the PC1 income gradient. Low-Income Churn spans a 4-unit PC1 range across its 80 DBSCAN nodes; High-Income Inactive spans 4 units across 164 nodes. Persistent homology cannot see this variation (it summarises global connectivity); flat clustering cannot see it (it assigns a single label). Mapper makes it navigable.

### 5.2 The Algorithm Matters: Noise and Bridge Nodes

Our sensitivity analysis reveals a cautionary finding for applied Mapper users. Two results that appeared to characterise the data — the 49% noise rate and the absence of bridge nodes — turn out to characterise the clustering algorithm.

Under DBSCAN (eps = 0.5), 49.3% of trajectories are excluded as noise and zero bridge nodes exist, suggesting that regime boundaries are geometrically sharp and churning trajectories are too dispersed to cluster. Under agglomerative clustering (threshold = 1.5), 100% of trajectories are covered and 359 bridge nodes span regime boundaries. The same data, the same cover, the same lens — different algorithm, different conclusions about noise and boundary structure.

This does not invalidate DBSCAN-based results. DBSCAN's noise exclusion is itself informative: the trajectories it excludes are those without eps-neighbours in their cover element, confirming that they are locally sparse relative to their region of the embedding. But the noise label should not be mistaken for a data property when it is an algorithm property. Reporting results under both DBSCAN and agglomerative clustering — as we recommend — provides a more complete picture.

### 5.3 Implications for Within-Regime Heterogeneity

The within-regime heterogeneity detected by Mapper is robust to all 24 configurations tested, confirming its status as a geometric property of the trajectory manifold. This has implications for both research and policy.

For research, it means that regime-level statistics (mean escape rate, mean income, mean employment share) are incomplete descriptors. The Low-Income Churn regime's 80 DBSCAN nodes span from PC1 ≈ −1.2 (near the Employed Mid boundary) to PC1 ≈ 2.8 (near the Inactive Low boundary). Individuals at these opposite ends of the same regime label occupy qualitatively different positions in trajectory space. Regression models that use regime membership as a predictor implicitly average over this variation.

For policy, the Mapper graph provides a cartographic tool. Colouring nodes by PC1 shows where within a disadvantaged regime individuals sit on the income gradient. Nodes near the Employed Mid boundary plausibly contain individuals whose churning reflects temporary disruption with ongoing labour market attachment; nodes near the Inactive Low boundary contain individuals drifting toward the absorbing inactivity state. These sub-populations may require different interventions, but a regime-level analysis treats them identically.

### 5.4 Coverage and the Churning Regimes

The coverage gap under DBSCAN (40–51%) concentrates in the churning regimes. Mixed Churn covers only 6% of its members (232 of 3,787); Low-Income Churn covers 37% (755 of 2,064). Under agglomerative clustering, these regimes are fully covered — the trajectories are not too dispersed to cluster, only too dispersed for DBSCAN at the tested eps values.

The practical implication is that DBSCAN-based Mapper graphs underrepresent precisely the populations of greatest policy interest. Disadvantaged and churning trajectories are the ones most likely to be DBSCAN noise, and therefore the ones excluded from the "causal geography" that Mapper is meant to provide. Agglomerative clustering recovers these populations, at the cost of producing much larger graphs (13,620–44,396 nodes) that are harder to visualise and interpret.

A pragmatic recommendation: use DBSCAN for initial visualisation and interpretive exploration (its sparser graphs are more readable), but supplement with agglomerative results for coverage statistics, bridge node analysis, and any claims about regime boundaries.

### 5.5 Comparison with Optimal Matching

OM produces flat clusters with no internal structure; the silhouette score of 0.215 at *k* = 7 (Author, 2026a) confirms internal heterogeneity. Mapper makes this heterogeneity explicit and navigable. Where OM says "these 2,064 trajectories all belong to the Low-Income Churn type", Mapper says "within this type, there are 80 geometrically distinct sub-regions (under DBSCAN, covering 37% of members) or over 5,000 (under agglomerative, covering 100%), with PC1 values spanning 4 units."

OM remains computationally efficient and well understood. Mapper is more complex and parameter-dependent, but our sensitivity analysis demonstrates robustness of the principal findings. The division of labour is straightforward: OM asks "how many types?", Mapper asks "what is inside each type?"

### 5.6 Limitations

Mapper is not a topological invariant: the graph depends on the lens function, cover, and clustering algorithm. Our sensitivity analysis, while broader than most published Mapper applications, does not exhaust the parameter space. In particular, we test only one cover resolution (n_cubes = 30, overlap = 0.5), having established its selection via prior grid search. The interaction between cover resolution and clustering algorithm remains unexplored.

The within-regime heterogeneity we detect is descriptive. The Mapper graph shows *where* outcomes differ but not *why*. Identifying mechanisms requires additional covariates (age, education, region) and potentially causal methods; the Mapper graph identifies the sub-populations for whom the distinction matters most, but does not explain it.

The analysis depends on the PCA-20D embedding from n-gram frequency vectors. The companion paper reports ARI = 0.33 between PCA-20D and UMAP-16D regime assignments, so finer-grained structure is partly embedding-dependent. Whether Mapper sub-regions are robust to alternative embeddings remains untested.

The agglomerative clustering graphs (13,620–44,396 nodes) are too large for effective visual inspection. We visualise only the 1,500 largest nodes (Figure 6, right panel). Alternative approaches — interactive tools, hierarchical node aggregation, or topological simplification — may be needed to make large agglomerative Mapper graphs interpretable.

## 6. Conclusion

Three findings from this analysis shift the methodological conversation.

First, within-regime heterogeneity is real and robust. Across all 24 Mapper configurations — two clustering algorithms, four lens functions, three parameter values each — the seven GMM regimes fragment into sub-regions with distinct positions along the PC1 income gradient. Low-Income Churn nodes span 4 units on PC1; the endpoints represent qualitatively different positions in trajectory space. This heterogeneity is not visible in the persistence diagram (which summarises global connectivity) or in the GMM labels (which assign a single type per trajectory).

Second, the choice of clustering algorithm within Mapper qualitatively affects two widely-reported results: noise rate and bridge node count. Under DBSCAN, half the sample is noise and no bridge nodes exist; under agglomerative clustering, coverage is complete and hundreds of bridge nodes span regime boundaries. The lesson extends beyond this dataset: Mapper papers that report noise rates or bridge statistics without testing alternative clustering algorithms may be reporting algorithm properties rather than data properties.

Third, the Mapper graph provides a navigable complement to persistent homology. Colouring nodes by continuous outcome variables transforms an abstract topological summary into a readable map of trajectory space. The companion paper establishes that topological structure exists and matters; this paper shows where within that structure outcomes diverge, and for whom.

The method generalises to any point cloud equipped with typological labels and outcome variables: health trajectories, educational pathways, cross-national mobility data. Mapper provides the cartographic infrastructure for a question that trajectory research has acknowledged but lacked the tools to answer systematically.
