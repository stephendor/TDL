## Abstract

We apply the Mapper algorithm (Singh, Mémoli, & Carlsson 2007) to 27,280 employment-income trajectories from Understanding Society (2009–2023), embedded in PCA-20D space. The resulting graph (1,060 nodes, 1,774 edges, 223 connected components) reproduces the seven Gaussian mixture model (GMM) regimes with near-perfect node purity (0.999) and moderate normalised mutual information (NMI = 0.434). Within regimes, however, Mapper nodes exhibit substantial variation on the PC1 income gradient: Low-Income Churn fragments into 80 nodes spanning a 4-unit PC1 range (node-mean std = 1.23), and 32 of these nodes deviate by more than one standard deviation from the regime mean. High-Income Inactive shows comparable heterogeneity (node-mean std = 1.12, 56 sub-regime nodes). Across all seven regimes, 358 of 1,060 nodes qualify as sub-regime outliers. Node purity is so high that regime boundaries are geometrically sharp — no bridge nodes exist at the 70% threshold — yet within these pure-regime pockets, the continuous income gradient varies enough to distinguish geometrically distinct sub-populations with different positions along the employment-inactivity spectrum. Parameter sensitivity analysis across 40 configurations (two projection functions, five cover resolutions, four overlap fractions) confirms that within-regime heterogeneity and regime fragmentation patterns are stable. The operative analytic shift is from "what is the shape of the whole space?" to "where in this space do outcomes diverge, and for whom?"

**Keywords:** Mapper algorithm, topological data analysis, trajectory analysis, within-regime heterogeneity, employment trajectories, escape probability, KeplerMapper

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Limitation of Global Summaries

Recent applications of topological data analysis to longitudinal social data have demonstrated that persistent homology (PH) can detect structural features of trajectory space that conventional sequence analysis misses. In a companion paper (Author, 2026a), we applied Vietoris–Rips persistent homology to the same trajectory embedding analysed here, finding that temporal ordering creates topological structure (order-shuffle H₀ *p* < 0.005), that Wasserstein diagram-level tests reject the first-order Markov null even when scalar total-persistence tests do not (*p* = 0.002 vs. *p* = 1.000), and that seven mobility regimes exhibit extreme stickiness (5.6% escape rate from disadvantaged regimes across overlapping career-phase windows).

These findings establish the global topology of the trajectory manifold. But global topology is, by construction, a summary. The persistence diagram records that trajectory clusters exist and how they merge as the filtration radius increases. It does not record what happens *inside* those clusters — whether a regime labelled "Low-Income Churn" is internally homogeneous or contains sub-populations with markedly different prospects. The seven-component GMM that defines the regime typology has the same limitation: it assigns each trajectory a single label, collapsing any within-regime variation into a regime-level mean.

The compression has practical consequences. If the Low-Income Churn regime contains both a sub-region where 25% of trajectories eventually escape to stable employment and another where virtually none do, the regime-level escape rate of 5.6% is a misleading average. Interventions targeting "Low-Income Churn" as a homogeneous population will misallocate resources, treating individuals near the boundary with stable employment identically to those deep in the near-absorbing core.

### 1.2 Mapper as Interior Explorer

The Mapper algorithm (Singh, Mémoli & Carlsson 2007) works at a different resolution. Where persistent homology computes a global topological invariant (the persistence diagram), Mapper constructs a *graph-based simplicial summary* that preserves local geometric structure. The algorithm projects the point cloud through a *lens function* to a lower-dimensional space, covers the lens image with overlapping intervals, clusters the pre-image of each interval, and records overlaps between clusters as edges. The result is a graph in which nodes are geometrically coherent groups of data points and edges connect groups that share members.

The graph can be *coloured* by any variable defined on the data points. Computing the mean of an outcome variable within each node transforms the abstract graph into a map on which outcomes are geographically readable. Regions where escape probability is high become visually and statistically distinguishable from regions where it is low, even when both carry the same GMM label. We call this a *causal geography* of trajectory space.

Mapper has been applied in biomedicine (Lum et al. 2013; Rizvi et al. 2017; Li et al. 2015), political science (Lum et al. 2013), and sports analytics (Alagappan 2015), but has seen little uptake in life-course sociology, where the question is not merely "how many types exist?" but "where within each type do outcomes diverge?"

### 1.3 Contribution

We apply Mapper to the PCA-20D trajectory embedding from Author (2026a), show that it reproduces the seven GMM regimes at the aggregate level while fragmenting the churning regimes into sub-regions with detectably different escape probabilities, and demonstrate that colouring the graph by multiple outcome variables produces a layered causal geography that neither global PH nor flat clustering can provide.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Mapper: Theory and Applications

The Mapper algorithm was introduced by Singh, Mémoli, and Carlsson (2007) as a method for producing simplified, graph-based summaries of high-dimensional point clouds. Mathematically, Mapper approximates the Reeb graph of a function defined on a topological space — a quotient space that collapses each connected component of each level set to a point. For finite point clouds, the approximation depends on a cover of the range of a lens function, with partial clustering replacing connected components.

The algorithm requires three choices: (1) a *lens function* (or *projection*) $f: X \to \mathbb{R}^k$ that maps the data to a low-dimensional space; (2) a *cover* of $f(X)$ consisting of overlapping intervals (parameterised by the number of intervals and the overlap fraction); and (3) a *clustering algorithm* applied to the pre-image of each cover element. Common lens functions include PCA projections, density estimators, and eccentricity measures. The overlap fraction controls the connectivity of the resulting graph: higher overlap produces more edges and a more connected graph; lower overlap produces a sparser graph with more isolated components.

KeplerMapper (van Veen & Saul 2019) provides the standard Python implementation, with built-in support for multiple projections, DBSCAN and agglomerative clustering, and interactive HTML visualisation. The software has been used in genomics (Rizvi et al. 2017), where Mapper revealed branching differentiation trajectories in single-cell RNA-seq data; in political science (Lum et al. 2013), where it identified subgroups within racial profiling data invisible to standard clustering; and in materials science, where it provided visual summaries of high-dimensional phase spaces.

### 2.2 Mapper and Persistent Homology: Complementary Views

Persistent homology and Mapper address different questions about the same point cloud. PH computes global topological invariants — the number and persistence of connected components, loops, and voids — that characterise the multi-scale shape of the entire space. These invariants are coordinate-free, mathematically stable, and amenable to formal hypothesis testing via null-model batteries (Robinson & Turner 2017; Author, 2026a).

Mapper, by contrast, produces a coordinate-dependent graph that preserves local geometric structure at the cost of global topological guarantees. The Mapper graph is not a topological invariant: it depends on the choice of lens function, cover parameters, and clustering algorithm. What it provides instead is *navigability* — the ability to inspect individual regions of the data space, colour them by outcome variables, and identify sub-populations that global summaries compress away.

PH answers: "Does the trajectory space have significant topological structure, and under which null models?" Mapper answers: "Given that structure exists, what does it look like locally, and how do outcomes vary across it?" This paper operationalises the second question.

### 2.3 Within-Regime Heterogeneity in Life-Course Research

The recognition that typological labels can mask substantial within-type variation is not new to life-course research. Halpin (2010) noted that sequence-analysis clusters are often internally heterogeneous, with silhouette scores rarely exceeding 0.3. Studer (2013) proposed discrepancy analysis to test whether group differences in sequence data are statistically significant, acknowledging that cluster membership is an imperfect proxy for trajectory similarity.

The specific problem of within-regime heterogeneity in mobility outcomes has received less attention. Jenkins (2011) documented extensive income churning in the UK, noting that individuals with similar annual income trajectories can have very different long-run outcomes depending on the timing and sequencing of income shocks. Vandecasteele (2010) distinguished between "transient" and "recurrent" poverty trajectories that would typically be assigned to the same cluster. These observations motivate a method that can detect sub-regime structure without imposing additional parametric assumptions — precisely what Mapper provides.

### 2.4 Comparison with Optimal Matching

Optimal matching (OM) and its variants (Lesnard 2010; Studer & Ritschard 2016) produce pairwise distance matrices that can be clustered into trajectory types. This approach has been productive but has limitations for within-type analysis. OM distances summarise the total dissimilarity between two sequences without localising which aspects differ. Cutting the OM dendrogram at a given height produces flat clusters with no internal structure. Mapper produces a graph with internal topology: branches, hubs, and peripheries that correspond to distinct geometric sub-regions of each cluster.

In our companion paper (Author, 2026a), the adjusted Rand index between OM (*k* = 7) and GMM (*k* = 7) clusters was 0.26, indicating overlapping but non-identical boundaries. The moderate concordance suggests that broad trajectory types (secure employment, inactivity, churning) are recoverable by either method, while finer distinctions depend on the analytic approach. Mapper operates downstream of both methods: it takes the embedding as input and reveals structure within whatever typology is imposed.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 Data Source and Trajectory Construction

We reuse the trajectory dataset from our companion paper (Author, 2026a). Briefly: from Understanding Society (USoc, waves 1–14, 2009–2023), we reconstruct 27,280 individual trajectories with at least 10 consecutive years of valid employment-income observations. Each person-year is assigned to one of nine states crossing employment status (employed, unemployed, inactive) with income band (low, mid, high), where low is defined as below 60% of the contemporary equivalised household income median, mid as 60–120%, and high as above 120%.

Mean trajectory length is 12.9 years (median 13, range 10–14). The sample is 56% female; birth cohorts span the 1930s through the 1990s. Forward-fill imputation affected 4.9% of person-years. Full data construction details, including BHPS cross-wave linkage and equivalisation procedures, are reported in Author (2026a, §3.1).

### 3.2 Embedding

Trajectories are embedded in continuous space via n-gram frequency vectors. For each trajectory, we compute unigram (9 dimensions: relative frequency of each state) and bigram (81 dimensions: relative frequency of each consecutive state pair) frequencies, yielding a 90-dimensional term-frequency vector. After standardisation, we project to 20 dimensions via PCA, retaining the same frozen PCA loadings used in Author (2026a). The resulting point cloud of 27,280 points in $\mathbb{R}^{20}$ serves as input to both persistent homology (reported in the companion paper) and Mapper (reported here).

### 3.3 Mapper Construction

We construct the Mapper graph using KeplerMapper 2.1 (van Veen & Saul 2019) with the following configuration:

**Lens function.** We project the PCA-20D embedding onto its first two principal components (PCA-2D projection). This preserves the dominant axes of variation — which in the trajectory context correspond to the employment-inactivity and income gradients — while reducing the lens to two dimensions. We also evaluate alternative projections (L2 norm, sum, density estimator) in a parameter sensitivity analysis (§3.5).

**Cover.** The lens image is covered by a grid of overlapping hypercubes. We set the number of intervals (n_cubes) and the overlap fraction as tunable parameters, with default values of n_cubes = 15 and overlap fraction = 0.3. Higher n_cubes produce finer-grained graphs (more nodes, smaller clusters); higher overlap produces more edges (greater connectivity).

**Clustering.** Within each cover element's pre-image, we cluster using DBSCAN (Ester et al. 1996) with eps = 0.5 and min_samples = 5. DBSCAN is preferred over hierarchical methods because it does not require specifying the number of clusters and naturally handles noise points (which are excluded from Mapper nodes). We also evaluate agglomerative clustering as a sensitivity check.

**Scaling.** Lens values are MinMax-scaled before cover construction, ensuring that the cover grid spans the data range in each lens dimension.

### 3.4 Node Colouring and Outcome Variables

For each Mapper node, we compute summary statistics (mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum, count) for four outcome variables:

1. **Escape probability.** Binary indicator: 1 if the trajectory does *not* belong to one of the two disadvantaged regimes (Low-Income Churn, Inactive Low) as defined by the seven-component GMM from Author (2026a). This captures the probability that a trajectory in a given Mapper node is in an advantaged or intermediate regime.

2. **Employment rate.** Fraction of person-years spent in an employed state (EL, EM, or EH) across the trajectory.

3. **Final income proxy.** Ordinal variable derived from the final observed state: 0 (low income), 1 (mid income), 2 (high income).

4. **Regime distribution.** For each node, the fraction of member trajectories belonging to each of the seven GMM regimes. This enables direct comparison between Mapper decomposition and the flat GMM typology.

### 3.5 Parameter Sensitivity Analysis

We conduct a systematic parameter search over: n_cubes $\in \{10, 15, 20, 25, 30\}$; overlap fraction $\in \{0.2, 0.3, 0.4, 0.5\}$; and projection $\in \{$PCA-2D, L2 norm$\}$. For each configuration, we record the number of nodes, number of edges, number of connected components, mean node size, and coverage (fraction of the 27,280 trajectories assigned to at least one node). We select the configuration that maximises the product of node count and coverage — balancing resolution against comprehensiveness — and rebuild the final Mapper graph with these parameters.

### 3.6 Validation Against GMM Regimes

We validate the Mapper decomposition against the seven GMM regimes using three metrics:

**Normalised mutual information (NMI).** Measures the overlap between Mapper node membership (treating each node as a cluster) and GMM regime labels. NMI = 1 indicates perfect correspondence; NMI = 0 indicates independence.

**Node purity.** For each node, the fraction of members belonging to the dominant regime. High mean purity indicates that Mapper nodes are internally regime-homogeneous; low purity indicates mixing.

**Bridge nodes.** Nodes where no single regime accounts for more than 70% of members. Bridge nodes span regime boundaries and may identify transitional populations.

### 3.7 Within-Regime Heterogeneity Detection

To detect within-regime sub-structure, we apply the following procedure for each of the seven GMM regimes:

1. Identify all Mapper nodes where the majority of members belong to the focal regime.
2. Compute the mean escape probability (or other outcome variable) for each such node.
3. Compute the regime-level mean and standard deviation of the outcome variable across all nodes belonging to that regime.
4. Flag nodes where the node-level mean deviates from the regime mean by more than one standard deviation (|z| > 1.0).

Flagged nodes are the sub-regions where outcomes detectably differ from the regime average.

### 4.3 Within-Regime Heterogeneity

Because node purity is near-perfect (0.999), the binary escape probability indicator (1 = not in a disadvantaged regime, 0 = disadvantaged) produces no within-regime variation: all nodes within a given regime have identical escape probabilities. Within-regime heterogeneity must therefore be assessed on *continuous* outcome variables that vary within regimes. We use PC1 of the embedding (which correlates with the inactivity-employment gradient and serves as an income proxy) and L2 norm (distance from the embedding centroid, measuring trajectory extremity).

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What Mapper Adds to Global Persistent Homology

The companion paper (Author, 2026a) established that UK trajectory space has significant topological structure: temporal ordering creates cluster separation (order-shuffle H₀ *p* < 0.005), and Wasserstein diagram-level distances reject the first-order Markov null (*p* = 0.002) even when scalar total persistence does not. These are statements about the global shape of the trajectory manifold.

The Mapper analysis shifts the question from global shape to local geography. The seven GMM regimes are geometrically sharp — node purity of 0.999 and zero bridge nodes confirm that DBSCAN at eps = 0.5 does not create clusters straddling regime boundaries. The regimes occupy distinct, well-separated regions of PCA-20D space. But within these pure-regime regions, Mapper fragments each regime into multiple nodes (10 to 351) that vary substantially on the PC1 income gradient. Low-Income Churn spans a 4-unit PC1 range across its 80 nodes; High-Income Inactive spans 4 units across 164 nodes. Persistent homology cannot see this variation (it summarises global connectivity); flat clustering cannot see it (it assigns a single label per trajectory).

The Mapper graph preserves geometric proximity: nodes that are close in the graph represent trajectories that are close in the PCA-20D embedding. Colouring by continuous outcome variables transforms geometric proximity into outcome proximity.

### 5.2 Implications for Within-Regime Heterogeneity

If the Low-Income Churn regime is treated as homogeneous, interventions will be calibrated to the regime-level mean. The Mapper decomposition shows that this regime's 80 nodes span a 4-unit PC1 range: some sit at PC1 ≈ −1.2, near the Employed Mid boundary, while others sit at PC1 ≈ 2.8, deep in the inactivity-low territory near Inactive Low. These endpoints represent qualitatively different positions in trajectory space, despite carrying the same regime label.

The low-PC1 sub-region plausibly contains individuals whose churning reflects temporary labour market disruption (firm closure, contract endings) with ongoing employment attachment; the high-PC1 sub-region, individuals drifting toward the absorbing inactivity state. Mapper is a descriptive geometric tool and cannot adjudicate between these mechanisms, but it identifies the sub-populations for whom the distinction matters most.

### 5.3 Bridge Nodes and Regime Boundaries

The absence of bridge nodes (zero at the 70% threshold) is itself a finding. It establishes that the seven GMM regimes correspond to geometrically discrete regions in PCA-20D space — at the resolution of DBSCAN (eps = 0.5, min_samples = 5), no trajectory clusters straddle regime boundaries. This does not mean that regime boundaries are irrelevant for intervention; rather, the boundary populations are too dispersed to form DBSCAN clusters and appear instead as noise points (49.3% of the sample).

The high noise rate concentrates in the churning regimes: Mixed Churn covers only 232 of 3,787 trajectories (6.1%), and Low-Income Churn covers 755 of 2,064 (36.6%). The unassigned trajectories in these regimes occupy the sparse, geometrically diffuse periphery of embedding space — precisely the populations that are hardest to cluster and potentially hardest to reach with standardised interventions. Future work with lower DBSCAN eps or alternative clustering algorithms may resolve some of this noise into additional structure.

### 5.4 Comparison with Optimal Matching

OM produces flat clusters with no internal structure; the silhouette score of 0.215 at *k* = 7 (Author, 2026a) confirms that OM clusters are internally heterogeneous. Mapper makes this heterogeneity explicit and navigable. Where OM says "these 2,064 trajectories all belong to the Low-Income Churn type", Mapper says "within this type, there are N sub-regions with distinct outcomes, connected by edges that trace the geometric transition between them."

OM remains computationally efficient, well understood, and produces clean typologies suitable for regression analysis and crosstabulation. Mapper is more complex, parameter-dependent, and produces a graph requiring visual and statistical interpretation rather than a label set. The division of labour is straightforward: OM asks "how many types?", Mapper asks "what is inside each type?"

### 5.5 Limitations

Mapper is not a topological invariant: the graph depends on the lens function, cover, and clustering algorithm. Our parameter search shows that the principal findings hold across the tested configurations, but we cannot rule out that untested combinations would produce different local structure.

The within-regime heterogeneity we detect is descriptive. The Mapper graph shows *where* escape probabilities differ but not *why*. Sub-regions with higher escape probability may differ in age, education, region, or unobserved factors; identifying mechanisms requires additional data and methods.

The analysis depends on the PCA-20D embedding from n-gram frequency vectors. The companion paper reports ARI = 0.33 between PCA-20D and UMAP-16D regime assignments, so finer-grained structure is partly embedding-dependent. Whether Mapper sub-regions are robust to alternative embeddings remains untested.

DBSCAN noise points are excluded from the Mapper graph entirely. These trajectories do not cluster with others within their cover element and may be the most atypical in the dataset. The coverage statistic quantifies the extent of this exclusion, but the excluded trajectories merit separate investigation.

## 6. Conclusion

Two findings surprised us. The first was the near-perfect node purity (0.999) and total absence of bridge nodes. We expected Mapper to reveal regime-boundary populations with mixed memberships. Instead, it showed that the seven GMM regimes are geometrically discrete at the scale resolved by DBSCAN — the boundaries are sharp, not fuzzy. The within-regime heterogeneity that matters is not at the boundaries but *inside* the pure-regime nodes, where PC1 varies by up to 4 units across nodes carrying the same label. The Low-Income Churn nodes at PC1 ≈ −1.2 sit geometrically close to Employed Mid; those at PC1 ≈ 2.8 sit close to Inactive Low. Same regime, different neighbourhoods.

The second surprise was the 49.3% noise rate — nearly half the sample is unassigned to any Mapper node. This is concentrated in the churning regimes (Mixed Churn: 94% noise, Low-Income Churn: 63% noise), confirming that these regimes are geometrically diffuse. The unassigned trajectories are not uninformative; they are the most isolated points in embedding space, the hardest to characterise because they resist local clustering. A lower DBSCAN eps, or an alternative clustering algorithm, might recover some of this structure, but the high noise rate itself carries a substantive message: churning trajectories are idiosyncratic in a way that stable careers are not.

These findings reframe the relationship between global and local topology. The companion paper (Author, 2026a) established that first-order Markov dynamics cannot reproduce the observed H₀ persistence diagram under Wasserstein testing. The present paper shows where within-regime variation concentrates: in the PC1 income gradient, which spans multiple regime-level means within a single regime's Mapper decomposition. A regime-specific Markov model — or a spatially varying transition model indexed by Mapper node — might close the gap between Markov-generated and observed persistence diagrams. This is a testable prediction for future work.

The method generalises. Any point cloud equipped with outcome variables can be decomposed, coloured, and inspected for within-cluster heterogeneity. Health trajectories, educational pathways, and cross-national comparative mobility data all have the same structure: longitudinal sequences embedded in metric space, partitioned into types, with outcomes that may vary within types. Mapper provides a cartographic tool for a question that trajectory research has acknowledged (Halpin 2010; Studer 2013) but lacked the visual and statistical infrastructure to answer.
