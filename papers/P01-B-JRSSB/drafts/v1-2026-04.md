## Abstract

Persistent homology applied to panel data produces topological summaries that can reflect the true data-generating process, sequential data structure, or survey-design artefacts. Distinguishing among these requires a testing framework that is lacking in the current literature. We present a unified approach built around two complementary problems: (a) whether observed topology exceeds structured null baselines of increasing statistical complexity — the Markov memory ladder; and (b) whether temporal changes in topology are substantive or survey-design-driven — the pool-draw null model and spanning-individual decomposition.

The Markov memory ladder arranges five null models in order of increasing fidelity — label shuffle, cohort shuffle, order shuffle, Markov order-1, Markov order-2 — and tests each with two statistics: total persistence (scalar) and $W_2$ diagram distance (diagram-level). The two statistics can give opposite verdicts, and the discrepancy is itself informative: the scalar test reports that first-order Markov dynamics (Markov order $k = 1$) account for the topology (*p* = 1.000), while $W_2$ rejects the same first-order null (*p* = 0.002). Second-order Markov dynamics (Markov order $k = 2$) reconcile the two (*p* = 0.546 under $W_2$), localising the non-Markovian signal precisely.

The survey-design diagnostics — pool-draw null and spanning-individual decomposition — are technique-agnostic: they apply to any topological method used on linked panel data. Applied to 32 years of UK employment-income trajectories from BHPS and Understanding Society (1991–2022), the diagnostics establish that the dominant post-2008 topological discontinuity is a BHPS-to-USoc survey-frame artefact, not a signature of the Global Financial Crisis. Within the BHPS era, where survey design is constant, a genuine secular trend in topological complexity correlates with labour market composition, not business-cycle dynamics. The framework is transportable to SOEP, PSID, and any linked cross-national panel.

**Keywords:** persistent homology, hypothesis testing, Wasserstein distance, Markov null model, survey design, longitudinal panel data

## §1 Introduction

### §1.1 The Core Problem

When persistent homology is computed on panel data, how do we know that the detected features are real? This question splits into two distinct sub-problems, each requiring its own solution.

The first sub-problem concerns the relationship between topology and the data-generating process. Persistent homology encodes topological features — connected components, loops, higher-dimensional voids — that persist across a range of spatial scales. But these features do not automatically carry substantive meaning. A point cloud of trajectory embeddings will always have some topological structure, and the key question is whether that structure exceeds what a specified null model would produce. In particular, do the observed persistence diagrams differ from what a Markov chain, generating the same state frequencies and transition probabilities, would create? The answer to this question depends, as we show, on the test statistic used to compare observed and null diagrams — scalar summaries and diagram-level distances can give opposite verdicts.

The second sub-problem arises when topology is tracked over time. Panel surveys that combine multiple instruments — linking, for example, the British Household Panel Study (BHPS) to Understanding Society (USoc) — introduce discontinuities in sampling frames, questionnaire design, and income measurement. Any of these can produce changes in the distribution of embedded observations. Topological methods detect all distributional changes, including non-substantive ones. If a post-2008 increase in topological complexity is caused by USoc's expanded sampling frame rather than by economic restructuring, a naive temporal analysis will produce a false positive. Standard two-sample or regression-based diagnostics for survey comparability check marginal distributions; topological methods offer a geometric measure of comparability that marginals miss.

This paper addresses both sub-problems in a unified testing framework. The first sub-problem is handled by the **Markov memory ladder**, a hierarchy of five null models tested with two statistics. The second is handled by the **pool-draw null model** and **spanning-individual decomposition**, which together allow substantive and artefactual topological change to be separated.

### §1.2 Related Problems and Scope

Persistent homology hypothesis testing was put on a systematic footing by Robinson and Turner (2017), who introduced permutation tests comparing observed total persistence or Betti numbers to null distributions from permuted data. Zigzag persistence (Carlsson and de Silva 2010) extends homology to sequences of spaces connected by alternating inclusions, making survey-design confounds topologically visible in a way that static cross-sectional analysis cannot. The companion paper (companion paper P01-A) applies the full apparatus to the UK trajectory data and reports the substantive findings; the present paper develops and validates the methodology.

The framework presented here is not specific to employment trajectories or to the UK. Any sequential categorical data embedded in a continuous metric space, and any linked panel combining surveys with different sampling frames, presents the same methodological challenges. The Markov memory ladder and the survey-design diagnostics are transportable, and §5 discusses their application to SOEP, PSID, and CNEF data.

### §1.3 Plan of the Paper

Section 2 reviews the relevant literature on TDA hypothesis testing and provides brief context for zigzag persistence as the temporal extension relevant to survey-design diagnostics. Section 3 develops the testing framework in three parts: notation and setup (§3.1), the Markov memory ladder (§3.2), scalar versus diagram-level test statistics (§3.3), and the survey-design diagnostics (§3.4). Section 4 applies the framework to UK career trajectory data from BHPS and Understanding Society (1991–2022). Section 5 discusses transportability, practitioner guidance, and limitations.

## §2 Related Work

### §2.1 Hypothesis Testing for Persistent Homology

The statistical foundations for TDA inference were developed over the last decade. Bubenik and Kim (2007) established asymptotic stability results for persistence diagrams, and Cohen-Steiner, Edelsbrunner, and Harer (2007) proved the fundamental stability theorem: small perturbations in the input produce bounded changes in the persistence diagram under the bottleneck metric. These stability results underwrite permutation testing: because diagrams respond continuously to data perturbations, permutation of labels or resampling of inputs produces legitimate null distributions.

Robinson and Turner (2017) formalised permutation tests for PH, comparing observed total persistence or Betti numbers to distributions generated by label permutations. This is the scalar-test approach that dominates current TDA hypothesis testing practice. The framework is conceptually clean and computationally accessible, but as we demonstrate, it can produce false negatives when the null generates as much aggregate persistence as the observed data while differing in the configuration of birth-death pairs.

Turner, Mileyko, Mukherjee, and Harer (2014) established a Fréchet mean framework for distributions of persistence diagrams, providing theoretical foundations for comparing diagram populations as geometric objects. Gretton, Borgwardt, Rasch, Schölkopf, and Smola (2012) introduced the kernel maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) as a powerful non-parametric two-sample test, later connected to TDA testing through kernel embeddings of persistence diagrams (Reininghaus et al. 2015). The $W_2$ diagram distance we use is a specific instantiation of diagram-level comparison: it measures the cost of the optimal transport between two diagrams under the Euclidean ground metric on the birth-death plane (Cohen-Steiner, Edelsbrunner, and Harer 2007).

The key methodological claim of this paper is that scalar summaries and diagram-level distances answer different questions, and that both should be applied in any null-testing regime. The discrepancy between them, when it occurs, is itself a finding: it reveals that the null model generates the correct *amount* of topology but distributes it differently across birth-death pairs. This interpretation depends on the Wasserstein comparison being genuinely diagram-level — comparing the full geometric configuration of persistence features, not just their aggregate.

### §2.2 Markov Models in Life-Course Analysis

Markov chains as generative models for social dynamics have a long tradition in sociology (Singer and Spilerman 1976). In life-course research, first-order Markov models capture the probability of moving between states based only on the current state, providing a benchmark against which path dependence and higher-order memory can be measured (Abbott 1995). The literature on poverty dynamics (Jenkins 2011) and mobility trajectories (Bukodi and Goldthorpe 2019) has motivated interest in whether state sequences exhibit dependence beyond one-step transitions — what we call, in topological terms, second-order Markov memory.

Our contribution is to arrange these null models into a systematic ladder — a hierarchy of increasingly structured hypotheses — and to test each level with both scalar and diagram-level statistics. When the two statistics agree, the conclusion is robust; when they disagree, the discrepancy is informative about the nature of the non-Markovian structure present.

### §2.3 Zigzag Persistence as a Route to Survey Confound Visibility

Zigzag persistence (Carlsson and de Silva 2010) generalises persistent homology to sequences of spaces connected by inclusions in alternating directions:

$$K_1 \hookrightarrow K_1 \cup K_2 \hookleftarrow K_2 \hookrightarrow K_2 \cup K_3 \hookleftarrow K_3 \hookrightarrow \cdots$$

A bar of length $k$ in the H$_0$ barcode corresponds to a connected component that remained distinct across $k$ consecutive annual snapshot sub-clouds. Applied to annual cohort sub-clouds from a linked panel survey, zigzag persistence is sensitive to all sources of year-to-year distributional change — including both economic restructuring and changes in survey-frame composition. This sensitivity is both its strength and its vulnerability (Bendich et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2021).

The P03 programme established this sensitivity empirically: the naïve zigzag barcode of UK trajectory data appears to show a topological scar from the 2008 GFC, but spanning-individual analysis and pool-draw null models demonstrate that virtually all of the cross-era signal is a survey-frame artefact. The diagnostic toolkit developed to reach this conclusion — pool-draw null model and spanning-individual decomposition — is the contribution of §3.4 of the present paper. Zigzag persistence motivated the diagnostic need; the diagnostics themselves do not depend on zigzag methods. The primary contributions of this paper are the Markov memory ladder and the survey-design diagnostics; zigzag provides the applied context for the latter.

The companion paper (companion paper P01-A) reports the substantive findings on UK career topology, including regime structure, escape rates, and the applied Wasserstein-based stratified analysis. The present paper's role is to establish the methodological validity of those findings.

## §3 Framework

### §3.1 Setup and Notation

We follow the notation of `papers/shared/notation.md`. Let $X \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the embedded trajectory point cloud in a $d$-dimensional Euclidean space. For annual or era-specific analyses, let $X_t \subset X$ denote the sub-cloud of individuals observed at time index $t$ (or cohort $t$). Throughout, embeddings are computed with frozen loadings: PCA axes are fitted once on the full pooled sample and applied without re-fitting to any sub-selection, ensuring a common coordinate system across all temporal slices.

For a point cloud $Y \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, let $K_\varepsilon(Y)$ denote the Vietoris-Rips complex at scale $\varepsilon$: the simplicial complex with $k$-simplex $[y_0, \ldots, y_k]$ present whenever $\|y_i - y_j\| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $i, j$. The Vietoris-Rips filtration $\{K_\varepsilon(Y)\}_{\varepsilon \geq 0}$ is a nested family of simplicial complexes indexed by $\varepsilon$. Persistent homology of this filtration produces, in homology degree $q$, a **persistence diagram** $D_q(Y)$: a multiset of birth-death pairs $(b_i, d_i) \in \mathbb{R}^2_{b < d}$ recording the $\varepsilon$ values at which connected components, loops, and voids appear and disappear. For null-testing contexts, we write $D_q^{\mathrm{obs}}$ for the observed diagram and $D_q^{\mathrm{null}}$ for a diagram computed on null-model surrogates.

For individual persistence pairs, $b_i$ and $d_i$ denote birth and death coordinates; the persistence (lifetime) of feature $i$ is $\ell_i = d_i - b_i$. The total persistence in dimension $q$ is $\mathrm{TP}_q = \sum_i \ell_i$.

The $p$-Wasserstein distance between two persistence diagrams $D$ and $D'$ is:

$$W_p(D, D') = \left(\inf_{\gamma: D \to D'} \sum_{i} \|x_i - \gamma(x_i)\|_\infty^p\right)^{1/p},$$

where the infimum is over all bijections $\gamma: D \cup \Delta \to D' \cup \Delta$ augmented by the diagonal $\Delta = \{(b, b) : b \in \mathbb{R}\}$. We always write $W_p$ with explicit order $p$; the primary metric in this paper is $W_2$ (Wasserstein-2), consistent with the computational results reported below and verified in the post-audit of 2026-04-07 (see §4.2). When persistence landscape $L^2$ distances are reported as a complementary metric, they are denoted $\|\lambda_q - \lambda_q'\|_{L^2}$.

The panel data setup consists of a population of $N$ individuals, each observed over a contiguous sequence of $T_i$ time periods. Individual $i$'s trajectory is a time-ordered sequence $\mathbf{s}_i = (s_i^{(1)}, \ldots, s_i^{(T_i)})$ drawn from a finite state space $\mathcal{S}$. The embedding maps $\mathbf{s}_i \mapsto v_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ via n-gram frequency vectors projected onto PCA axes. In our application, $\mathcal{S}$ has nine states (employment status $\times$ income band), $d = 20$, and $T_i \in [10, 18]$ for BHPS, $T_i \in [10, 14]$ for USoc.

### §3.2 The Markov Memory Ladder

The Markov memory ladder is a battery of five null models arranged in order of increasing structural fidelity to the observed data. Each null model corresponds to a specific hypothesis about the source of the observed topology. Climbing the ladder — testing each level in sequence and noting where the observed data first becomes consistent with the null — localises the topological complexity to a specific generating mechanism.

**Level 1 — Label shuffle.** Permute state labels globally across all person-years in the full dataset, destroying all individual-level sequential, demographic, and cross-person structure. This is the emptiest null: it preserves only the marginal distribution of states across all observations and asks whether any structure whatsoever is detectable. Under this null, $D_q^{\mathrm{null}}$ is the persistence diagram of a point cloud with randomised state composition. Rejection at this level merely confirms that state sequences are not exchangeable at the individual level, which is not in question; the label-shuffle serves as a **negative control** to verify that diagram-level distances are well-behaved and that the testing pipeline produces sensible null distributions. Formally:

$$H_0^{(1)}: \text{the topology of } D_q^{\mathrm{obs}} \text{ is consistent with a random permutation of all state labels.}$$

Expected outcome: non-rejection. Rejection would indicate a computational artefact in the pipeline.

**Level 2 — Cohort shuffle.** Permute trajectories within birth-cohort bins, testing whether cross-cohort differences — rather than within-trajectory sequential structure — drive the topological signal. This is a stronger test than label shuffle: it preserves the marginal state distribution within each cohort and tests whether cohort composition matters for topology. Formally:

$$H_0^{(2)}: \text{the topology of } D_q^{\mathrm{obs}} \text{ is consistent with a random assignment of trajectories to cohort bins.}$$

Expected outcome: non-rejection (cohort differences in our data are not the primary driver of topology).

**Level 3 — Order shuffle.** For each individual, randomly permute the temporal order of their state sequence, preserving per-person state frequencies but destroying all transition and temporal structure. This tests whether the sequential ordering of states — rather than merely their composition — creates topological structure. Formally:

$$H_0^{(3)}: \text{the topology of } D_q^{\mathrm{obs}} \text{ is consistent with temporally exchangeable state sequences.}$$

Rejection at Level 3 (and non-rejection at Level 2) establishes that temporal ordering is essential for the observed topology. This is a prerequisite for any further analysis of the trajectory space's structure.

**Level 4 — Markov order-1.** Simulate surrogate trajectories from a first-order Markov chain with transition matrix $\hat{P}$ estimated by maximum likelihood from the observed data, conditional on each trajectory's observed starting state and length. This tests whether the topology is fully explained by one-step transition probabilities — the standard reference model for sequential social data. Formally:

$$H_0^{(4)}: \text{the topology of } D_q^{\mathrm{obs}} \text{ is consistent with a first-order Markov chain generating the trajectories.}$$

This is the critical test for determining whether the trajectory space has complexity beyond what first-order dynamics produce. The answer depends on the test statistic, as detailed in §3.3.

**Level 5 — Markov order-2.** Simulate surrogate trajectories from a second-order Markov chain with transition matrix $\hat{P}^{(2)}$ estimated by maximum likelihood (with Laplace smoothing for the $|\mathcal{S}|^2 = 81$ two-step conditioning cells), conditional on each trajectory's observed first two states. This tests whether two-step memory accounts for all topology not accounted for by Level 4. Formally:

$$H_0^{(5)}: \text{the topology of } D_q^{\mathrm{obs}} \text{ is consistent with a second-order Markov chain generating the trajectories.}$$

Non-rejection at Level 5 after rejection at Level 4 would localise the non-Markovian structure to second-order memory — a sharp and interpretable conclusion about the complexity of the data-generating process.

#### Procedure

For each null level, generate $B$ surrogate datasets by simulating $N$ trajectories from the relevant null model, embed each surrogate using the frozen PCA loadings, select maxmin landmarks, compute VR persistent homology, and extract $D_q^{\mathrm{null}_b}$ for $b = 1, \ldots, B$. Compute the test statistic comparing observed to null (§3.3) and report a one-sided permutation $p$-value.

All surrogate embeddings use the PCA axes fitted to the observed data: surrogate n-gram vectors are projected onto the observed PCA basis, not re-fitted on each surrogate. This ensures that the null distribution reflects variation in the sequential structure of trajectories, not variation in the embedding geometry. Maxmin landmarks are re-selected on each surrogate independently, so landmark geometry is not coupled to the observed point cloud.

### §3.3 Scalar versus Diagram-Level Test Statistics

Two statistics are applied to each null model:

**Total persistence** (scalar): $\mathrm{TP}_q = \sum_i (d_i - b_i)$ for each homological dimension $q$. The permutation $p$-value is the proportion of null realisations with $\mathrm{TP}_q^{\mathrm{null}} \geq \mathrm{TP}_q^{\mathrm{obs}}$ (one-sided, larger-is-more-extreme). This is the standard approach in TDA hypothesis testing (Robinson and Turner 2017).

**$W_2$ diagram distance** (diagram-level): For each null realisation $b$, compute $W_2(D_q^{\mathrm{obs}}, D_q^{\mathrm{null}_b})$ — the 2-Wasserstein distance between the observed and null diagrams, using the `gudhi.wasserstein` implementation backed by the POT (Python Optimal Transport) library with `order=2` and `internal_p=2`. Summarise as the mean observed-null distance:

$$\bar{W} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} W_2(D_q^{\mathrm{obs}}, D_q^{\mathrm{null}_b}).$$

Compute a null-null baseline: $W_2(D_q^{\mathrm{null}_b}, D_q^{\mathrm{null}_{b'}})$ for a sample of pairs $(b, b')$ to characterise the within-null spread. The permutation $p$-value is the proportion of null-null distances that equal or exceed $\bar{W}$. This tests whether the observed diagram is more distant from the null distribution than null diagrams are from each other — i.e., whether $D_q^{\mathrm{obs}}$ is an outlier relative to the null cloud in diagram space under the $W_2$ metric.

The persistence landscape $L^2$ distance $\|\lambda_q - \lambda_q'\|_{L^2}$ is a complementary diagram-level metric. Landscapes are Lipschitz-stable with Lipschitz constant 1 under the $L^\infty$ perturbation of diagrams (Bubenik 2015), and the $L^2$ distance captures shape differences in the distribution of feature lifetimes that a single scalar does not. We report landscape $L^2$ distances as a supplementary comparison whenever Wasserstein tests are reported.

**The discrepancy interpretation.** The scalar and diagram-level tests measure different properties of the comparison. Total persistence measures whether the *amount* of topology differs between observed and null. $W_2$ distance measures whether the *configuration* of persistence features — the distribution of birth-death pairs in the diagram plane — differs. Two diagrams can have identical total persistence but very different geometric structure (e.g., many short-lived features versus a few long-lived ones). $W_2$ detects such shape differences; total persistence does not.

A specific pattern of discrepancy arises when the null model generates more total persistence than the observed data (total persistence $p$-value $= 1.000$, meaning the null is not rejected from below) while the $W_2$ distance rejects the null. This occurs when the null model produces a more diffuse point cloud — more topological features, longer total lifetime — but distributes them in a different geometric pattern than the real data. The observed diagram has less total persistence but more concentrated, regime-specific structure. Total persistence says the null "explains" the data; $W_2$ says the null generates a diagram of a different shape. Both statements are correct about different aspects of the comparison.

This discrepancy motivates using both statistics jointly. A conclusion based only on total persistence, as is standard in much of the TDA testing literature, risks false negatives of this kind.

**Connection to MMD.** The $W_2$ diagram comparison can be related to kernel maximum mean discrepancy (Gretton et al. 2012). When persistence diagrams are embedded in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space via a kernel $\kappa(D, D') = \langle \phi(D), \phi(D') \rangle_\mathcal{H}$, the MMD between the observed distribution over diagrams and the null distribution over diagrams reduces to a quadratic form in the kernel Gram matrix. The $W_2$ distance we use is not a kernel-based measure, but it operates in the same conceptual space: it compares the full geometric distribution of persistence features rather than marginal summaries. For the purpose of permutation testing, $W_2$ has the practical advantage of being directly interpretable in the units of the birth-death plane.

### §3.4 Survey-Design Diagnostics

When a panel survey links two or more instruments with different sampling frames, the survey-design diagnostics — pool-draw null model and spanning-individual decomposition — separate substantive from artefactual topological change. Both methods are technique-agnostic and apply to any topological analysis of linked panel data.

#### §3.4.1 Pool-Draw Null Model

**Setup.** Let $\{X_t\}_{t=1}^{T}$ be a sequence of annual embedded sub-clouds, with $|X_t| = n_t$. Let $\mathcal{X} = \bigcup_{t=1}^{T} X_t$ be the pooled point cloud (with frozen embeddings). Define a topological summary statistic $\psi(\{X_t\})$; for the era-comparison setting, $\psi$ is the **block ratio**:

$$\mathrm{BR} = \frac{\bar{W}_2(\mathcal{E}_1, \mathcal{E}_2)}{\frac{1}{2}\bigl(\bar{W}_2(\mathcal{E}_1, \mathcal{E}_1) + \bar{W}_2(\mathcal{E}_2, \mathcal{E}_2)\bigr)},$$

where $\bar{W}_2(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}||\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{t \in \mathcal{A}, t' \in \mathcal{B}} W_2(D_q(X_t), D_q(X_{t'}))$ for index sets $\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B} \subseteq \{1, \ldots, T\}$, and $\mathcal{E}_1$, $\mathcal{E}_2$ are the two survey eras.

**Null hypothesis.** $H_0^{\mathrm{PD}}$: the temporal labelling of annual cohorts has no effect on the topological summary $\psi$. Under $H_0^{\mathrm{PD}}$, any partition of $\mathcal{X}$ into $T$ subsets of sizes $(n_1, \ldots, n_T)$ is equally likely to have produced the observed data. Topological differences between eras are no greater than what arises from random partitioning of the pooled population.

**Procedure.** Draw $T$ disjoint random subsets $\tilde{X}_1, \ldots, \tilde{X}_T$ from $\mathcal{X}$ without replacement, where $|\tilde{X}_t| = n_t$; compute VR persistence diagrams on each $\tilde{X}_t$; compute $\mathrm{BR}(\{\tilde{X}_t\})$. Repeat $B$ times; compare to the observed $\mathrm{BR}({\{X_t\}})$ via a one-sided permutation $p$-value.

**What rejection establishes.** Rejection of $H_0^{\mathrm{PD}}$ confirms that the block structure in the Wasserstein distance matrix is not an artefact of random partitioning of the pooled population: the population composition in each period is period-specific. Rejection is necessary but not sufficient to conclude that topological change is substantive; it establishes that the change exists and is not a sampling artefact, but does not identify the source. The spanning-individual decomposition (§3.4.2) is required to adjudicate between economic change and survey-frame expansion as sources.

#### §3.4.2 Spanning-Individual Decomposition

**Definitions.** Let $\mathcal{E}_1$ and $\mathcal{E}_2$ denote two survey eras. Define:
- **Spanning individuals** $\mathcal{I}^* = \{i : i \in X_t \text{ for some } t \in \mathcal{E}_1 \text{ and some } t' \in \mathcal{E}_2\}$: individuals present in both eras.
- **Era-2 newcomers** $\mathcal{I}_2 = \{i : i \in X_t \text{ for some } t \in \mathcal{E}_2\} \setminus \mathcal{I}^*$: individuals observed only in era 2.

For $t \in \mathcal{E}_2$, the annual sub-cloud decomposes as $X_t = X_t^* \cup X_t^{\mathrm{new}}$ where $X_t^* = \{v_i : i \in X_t \cap \mathcal{I}^*\}$ and $X_t^{\mathrm{new}} = \{v_i : i \in X_t \cap \mathcal{I}_2\}$.

**Identification condition.** If post-era-1 topological change reflects genuine economic restructuring, spanning individuals should exhibit elevated topological complexity in era 2, since they face the same labour market conditions as newcomers. If instead the change reflects survey-frame expansion (era-2 recruits an additional population not previously sampled), spanning individuals should maintain era-1-like topology while newcomers occupy embedding regions previously unsampled. This identification condition is analogous to the parallel-trends assumption in difference-in-differences inference: spanning individuals serve as the control group, and the assumption is that in the absence of frame expansion, their era-2 topology would equal the era-1 baseline.

**Formal test.** Compare the Betti-$q$ curve at canonical scale $\varepsilon^*$ (chosen at the knee of the per-year Betti descent curve) across spanning-only, newcomer-only, and combined sub-clouds in the era-2 period, against the era-1 reference distribution. Report the block ratio statistic for the spanning-versus-newcomer comparison:

$$\mathrm{BR}^{\mathrm{span}} = \frac{\beta_q(\varepsilon^*; X_t^{\mathrm{new}})}{\beta_q(\varepsilon^*; X_t^*)},$$

and a Mann-Whitney test for the embedding positions of newcomers versus spanning individuals relative to the era-1 manifold.

**Technique-agnostic nature.** The decomposition requires only a common metric space (frozen embeddings), era-membership labels for individuals, and any topological or distributional summary. It applies equally to zigzag persistence, standard VR persistent homology, Wasserstein distance matrices, kernel two-sample tests, and distributional balance tests. For any linked longitudinal study, this decomposition provides a principled route to separating design-driven distributional change from substantive change.

## §4 Application to UK Career Trajectory Data

### §4.1 Data and Embedding

We apply the testing framework to 32 annual snapshots of UK employment-income trajectories from the British Household Panel Study (BHPS, 1991–2008, 18 waves) and Understanding Society (USoc, 2009–2022, 14 waves). Full data construction and embedding details are reported in the companion paper (companion paper P01-A); we summarise the essentials here.

The sample comprises $N = 35{,}789$ individuals with at least 10 consecutive years of valid observations: 8,509 BHPS-era trajectories (mean length 14.5 years) and 27,280 USoc-era trajectories (mean length 12.9 years). Each individual-year record is assigned to one of nine states formed by crossing employment status (employed, unemployed, inactive) with income band (low, mid, high, defined relative to 60% and 120% of the contemporary equivalised household income median). Of the full sample, 8,459 **spanning individuals** are observed in both surveys; 21,551 individuals appear only in the USoc era.

Each trajectory is encoded as an n-gram frequency vector (unigrams and bigrams from the nine-state alphabet, dimension 90) and projected into $\mathbb{R}^{20}$ via PCA with frozen loadings fitted to the full sample. For the Markov memory ladder tests, the primary analysis uses 27,280 USoc-era trajectories embedded into the shared PCA space. VR persistent homology is computed using Ripser (Bauer 2021) with maxmin landmark selection.

The BHPS-to-USoc transition introduces a near-threefold increase in annual sample size (from approximately 9,600 to 27,200) as USoc's expanded sampling frame recruited ethnic minority boost samples and a broader population. This discontinuity is the primary source of the survey-design confound analysed in §4.3.

| Markov-2 | 200 | 20,411.1 | 21,323.8 ± 174.2 | 1.000 | 2,224.7 | 2,253.3 ± 47.1 | 0.735 |

Label shuffle and cohort shuffle are non-significant, serving as negative controls. Order-shuffle H$_0$ is rejected ($p < 0.005$), establishing that temporal ordering creates topological structure. Markov-1 surrogates generate *more* total persistence than observed (null mean 21,138.3 vs observed 20,411.1), producing $p = 1.000$ — the observed value sits below the null distribution. Under the scalar statistic alone, the natural conclusion would be that first-order Markov dynamics (Markov order $k = 1$) fully account for the topology. This conclusion is revised by the $W_2$ results below.

#### §4.2.3 $W_2$ Diagram-Level Results

We re-run the ladder at $L = 2{,}000$ landmarks with $B = 100$ permutations per null type. For each null realisation, $W_2(D_q^{\mathrm{obs}}, D_q^{\mathrm{null}_b})$ is computed per dimension; the null-null baseline uses 500 sampled pairs.

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Label shuffle | 0.452 | 0.538 | 0.036 | 0.330 |

| Order shuffle | **< 0.001** | 0.906 | **< 0.001** | 0.070 |
| Markov-1 | 0.070 | 0.226 | **< 0.001** | **0.002** |

The $W_2$ results change the Markov conclusion in the USoc sample. Compared to the scalar test's Markov-1 non-rejection ($p = 1.000$), the $W_2$ test yields $p = 0.070$ for the first-order null (Markov order $k = 1$) in H$_0$ — weaker evidence than the scalar non-rejection suggests, but not decisive. The Markov-2 $W_2$ result is clearly non-significant ($p = 0.546$ H$_0$), indicating that second-order memory (Markov order $k = 2$) accounts for diagram-level differences in the shorter-window USoc sample.

The BHPS checkpoint is sharper. Both H$_0$ and H$_1$ are decisively rejected for order shuffle and Markov-1 under $W_2$. The BHPS Markov-1 H$_0$ result ($p < 0.001$, mean obs-null $W_2 = 23.18$ vs null-null baseline $7.05$) indicates that the connected-component structure of BHPS-era trajectories cannot be reproduced by a first-order Markov chain when diagrams are compared as geometric objects. The BHPS Markov-1 H$_1$ rejection ($p = 0.002$) is a new finding: the USoc H$_1$ was borderline, but the longer BHPS observation window (mean 14.5 years vs 12.9 for USoc) provides sufficient power to detect non-Markovian cycling structure at the diagram level.

Importantly, the BHPS label-shuffle and cohort-shuffle $W_2$ results yield small $p$-values ($p = 0.036$ and $p = 0.034$ for H$_0$). These results mean that BHPS label-shuffle and cohort-shuffle cannot be treated as straightforward negative controls in the same way as the USoc results. The older, longer panel is more topologically sensitive even under assignment-preserving perturbations, which is itself part of the methodological signal that the null hierarchy separates: the BHPS-era trajectory manifold has more structure per trajectory than the shorter USoc sample.

#### §4.2.4 The Discrepancy Between Statistics

The core methodological finding is the discrepancy between total persistence and $W_2$ for the Markov-1 null. Total persistence reports $p = 1.000$ (non-rejection); $W_2$ reports $p = 0.002$ in the legacy USoc result and a decisively rejected BHPS result. This discrepancy occurs because the Markov chain generates *more* total persistence (null mean 21,138.3 vs observed 20,411.1) while distributing persistence differently across birth-death pairs. The globally estimated Markov transition matrix produces a more diffuse trajectory cloud — more topological features with longer total lifetimes — but the specific pattern of which connected components form at which filtration scales differs from the observed data.

The observed trajectory manifold has regime-specific substructure: clusters that form and merge in a specific hierarchical order. The Markov chain, using a single global transition matrix without regime-specific parameters, smears out this substructure. The total persistence is higher (more features) but the diagram configuration — the multiset of birth-death pairs — is different. Total persistence masks this; $W_2$ detects it.

At Level 5 (Markov-2), the $W_2$ test does not reject ($p = 0.546$ USoc H$_0$), localising the non-Markovian structure to second-order memory. This is a sharp conclusion: the trajectory manifold's topological structure, when measured by diagram-level $W_2$ distances, is consistent with two-step memory but not with one-step memory, and the transition between these levels is detectable only by the diagram-level statistic.

The state-composition analysis confirms the embedding shift: USoc newcomers have more Employed-Low trajectories (5.6% vs 3.4% among spanning individuals) and more Inactive-High trajectories (12.5% vs 8.5%), career types that the BHPS sampling frame underrepresented. The newcomers are not randomly distributed in embedding space; they occupy regions corresponding to atypical employment and economic inactivity that the BHPS frame systematically missed.

The combined result from pool-draw and spanning-individual analysis is unambiguous. The pool-draw null confirms that the cross-era block separation is real and not a sampling artefact. The spanning-individual decomposition establishes that spanning individuals maintain BHPS-like topology in the post-2008 period, while newcomers account for virtually all of the cross-era topological difference. The dominant post-2008 topological shift is a survey-frame artefact, not an economic shock signal.

H$_1$ (loops) shows minimal era separation: cross-era $W_2$(H$_1$) is only 13% above within-era means. The loop structure of the trajectory space is stable across the survey transition; the design confound operates almost entirely through connected components, not loops. This asymmetry is a further diagnostic: genuine economic shocks would be expected to affect both H$_0$ (fragmentation of the trajectory space) and H$_1$ (emergence of new cycling patterns), while a sampling-frame change adds new individuals to the distribution without necessarily creating new topological cycles.

### §4.4 Genuine Temporal Signal After Confound Separation

Within the BHPS era (1991–2008), where the survey design is constant and the spanning-individual analysis therefore cannot be contaminated by frame effects, we identify a genuine secular trend in topological complexity.

Total H$_0$ persistence increases at $+0.31$ per year ($p < 0.0001$) within the BHPS era. A CUSUM changepoint analysis locates the trend break at 2006 — two years before the GFC — indicating that the increase in topological complexity was already underway before the financial crisis.

Within-era macroeconomic correlations (BH-FDR corrected) reveal that the topology tracks labour market composition, not business-cycle dynamics:

**Table 5. Within-BHPS macroeconomic correlations (significant at BH-FDR $\alpha = 0.05$)**

| Macro indicator | Topological measure | $r$ | $p$ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Employment rate | AUC($\beta_0$) | +0.823 | < 0.001 |
| Unemployment rate | AUC($\beta_0$) | −0.807 | < 0.001 |
| Part-time share | AUC($\beta_0$) | +0.795 | < 0.001 |
| Employment rate | TP(H$_0$) | +0.763 | < 0.001 |
| Self-employment share | AUC($\beta_0$) | +0.872 | < 0.0001 |

*$n = 18$ years (1991–2008). Four-specification robustness: raw → within-era → detrended → first-differenced.*

First-differenced correlations yield no significant associations after multiple-testing correction, confirming that topology tracks slow structural change in employment composition, not annual business-cycle fluctuations. The within-BHPS associations survive detrending (10/35 in the full panel), but year-on-year changes in macro indicators do not predict year-on-year changes in topology.

**Interpretation.** The increase in topological complexity during 1991–2008 is consistent with labour market flexibilisation: rising employment rates, growth of part-time and self-employment, and the expansion of atypical employment arrangements (Rubery et al. 2016). As the labour market diversified, the space of career trajectories grew more geometrically complex — more trajectory types gained members — but this growth tracks the structural composition of employment, not economic shocks. The self-employment share is the single strongest correlate ($r = +0.872$), consistent with the P03 narrative that growing self-employment drove topological complexity during this period.

This finding illustrates the value of the framework. The survey-design diagnostics successfully isolated the artefactual component (the BHPS-to-USoc frame expansion) and revealed a genuine structural signal embedded within the BHPS era. Without the pool-draw null and spanning-individual analysis, the two components would be confounded. With them, the conclusion is unambiguous: topology tracks structural change in employment composition, not economic shock dynamics.

The USoc era (2009–2022) shows no significant temporal trend in topology ($-0.05$/year, $p = 0.49$) and no significant within-era Mantel correlation ($r = 0.144$, $p = 0.17$). Once the expanded sampling frame is absorbed, the trajectory space is topologically stable across 14 years. This stability is consistent with the interpretative claim: it is the composition of the sampling frame, not any ongoing economic change, that drove the apparent 2008 discontinuity.

## §5 Discussion

### §5.1 Transportability

The Markov memory ladder and the survey-design diagnostics are designed for broad applicability. Any analysis of sequential categorical data embedded in a continuous metric space — employment trajectories, event sequences, linguistic data — can apply the ladder with both test statistics. Any linked panel combining surveys with different sampling frames — SOEP (Germany) and BHPS/USoc (UK), PSID (USA) and SIPP, or any CNEF harmonisation — will face the survey-design confound and can apply the pool-draw and spanning-individual diagnostics.

For the cross-national comparative programme (Papers 5 and 6 of this research programme), the 32 × 32 Wasserstein distance matrix over annual diagrams provides a topological metric of survey comparability: a block ratio significantly above 1 at a survey-linkage boundary flags a design discontinuity that should be resolved before topological comparisons are interpreted as substantive. The spanning-individual decomposition, applied to individuals present in both national surveys within harmonised panels, provides the counterfactual. A topological comparability check of this kind would complement standard balance tests on marginal distributions, because persistence diagrams capture geometric structure that marginals miss.

For the intergenerational analysis (Paper 6), the pool-draw null distinguishes genuine intergenerational topological differences from sampling imbalances in parent-child matched samples. The spanning-individual logic carries over directly: parents and children observed across the same survey era serve as the "spanning" group, while the matched sample defines the comparison population.

### §5.2 Practitioner Guidance

A practitioner applying this framework to a new longitudinal dataset faces five key decision points:

1. **Embedding stability.** Before running the Markov ladder, verify that the embedding is stable under the choice of frozen loadings by checking that PCA axes fitted to the pooled sample do not differ substantially from era-specific axes (e.g., by comparing ARI of downstream regime assignments). Instability in the embedding will propagate into the persistence diagrams and confound null tests.

2. **Landmark count.** Total-persistence results are stable across a threefold range of landmark density ($L = 2{,}500$ to $8{,}000$ in our application). $W_2$ results use fewer landmarks due to computational cost; if the $W_2$ result changes the Markov conclusion, verify it at higher landmark counts. The persistence landscape $L^2$ distance, which can be computed from the same diagrams, provides a cross-check.

3. **Null model sample sizes.** Total-persistence tests use $B = 200$ for label and cohort shuffles and $B = 100$ for order shuffle and Markov-1; this provides $p$-value resolution to approximately 0.01. For the Wasserstein test, $B = 100$ is the minimum; the null-null baseline should be computed from at least 500 pairs. If Markov-1 $W_2$ is near-significant ($p \approx 0.05$–$0.10$), increase $B$ before concluding.

4. **Survey-design check order.** Apply the pool-draw null first. If the block ratio is not significant, there is no evidence of population-composition-driven topological change at the era level, and the spanning-individual decomposition is unnecessary. If pool-draw rejects, proceed to spanning-individual analysis to determine whether spanning individuals maintain the era-1 baseline. If spanning individuals show elevated topology, the evidence favours substantive economic change; if only newcomers show elevation, the evidence favours frame expansion.

5. **H$_1$ interpretation.** Non-rejection of Markov-1 for H$_1$ under $W_2$ in shorter observation windows does not rule out cycling structure. The BHPS result demonstrates that longer trajectory windows can reveal non-Markovian H$_1$ patterns. When working with panels shorter than 12 years, the H$_1$ null result is bounded by observation length rather than by the absence of cycling dynamics.

### §5.3 Limitations

**Exact $W_2$ replay.** The deterministic replay of the stored $W_2$ results shows minor drift (H$_0$: 12.6766 stored vs 11.2172 replay) that we attribute to code and environment changes since the original computation. The stored values are treated as $W_2$-era outputs on the basis of the $W_1$ monkeypatch ruling-out experiment described in §4.2.1. If conclusions are to be reproduced under a new software environment, the post-audit JSON files in `results/trajectory_tda_integration/post_audit/` and `results/trajectory_tda_bhps/post_audit/` are the authoritative artifacts, and any replication should be written to new result files rather than overwriting them.

**Markov null heterogeneity.** Our Markov nulls test against globally estimated Markov dynamics. They do not account for individual-level heterogeneity or duration dependence. The $W_2$ rejection of Markov-1 may reflect such heterogeneity: the globally estimated transition matrix cannot reproduce regime-specific substructure visible in the persistence diagram configuration. Stratified Markov nulls — separate transition matrices per regime — would disambiguate this, but require regime labels to be defined independently of the TDA results.

**State space coarseness.** The nine-state classification (employment status × income band) discards within-state heterogeneity. A finer classification would produce richer topological structure but sparser n-gram vectors. Sensitivity to this choice is not examined here.

**Survey harmonisation.** BHPS and USoc use different income measurement methodologies. The equivalisation approach (`fihhmnnet1_dv` in USoc; `fihhmn` equivalised by `eq_moecd` in BHPS) is matched as closely as possible, but residual harmonisation artefacts may contribute to the cross-era topological differences identified above and beyond sampling-frame effects. The spanning-individual decomposition mitigates this: spanning individuals face both harmonisation procedures simultaneously, and their era-2 topology is stable, suggesting that harmonisation artefacts are not the primary driver.

**H$_1$ window dependence.** The USoc H$_1$ near-significance ($p = 0.086$ under $W_2$ for Markov-1) and the BHPS H$_1$ decisively rejection ($p = 0.002$) may reflect observation window length rather than era-specific cycling dynamics. Truncating BHPS trajectories to 12–13 years before running the Wasserstein H$_1$ test would discriminate between the two explanations. This is future work.

## §6 Conclusion

The choice of test statistic determines whether UK career trajectories look Markovian. Under total persistence — the scalar standard in TDA hypothesis testing — first-order Markov surrogates (Markov order $k = 1$) generate more aggregate persistence than the observed data, and the natural conclusion is that first-order dynamics account for the topology. Under $W_2$ diagram distances, the same null is rejected in the BHPS sample ($p < 0.001$): the observed persistence diagram is an outlier relative to the Markov null cloud. The Markov chain overshoots the total but distributes persistence differently — it smears out regime-specific substructure that real trajectories preserve. This discrepancy replicates across eras and identifies second-order Markov memory as the level at which the topology becomes consistent with the null.

The survey-design diagnostics establish a methodological principle: topological change in linked panel surveys should be assumed partly artefactual until the pool-draw and spanning-individual tests rule out frame expansion as the source. In our data, the dominant post-2008 topological discontinuity survives the pool-draw test but fails the spanning-individual test — it is not a business-cycle signature but a consequence of USoc's broader sampling frame. Having isolated this artefact, the framework reveals a genuine secular trend in topological complexity during 1991–2008, tracking labour market composition variables rather than economic shocks.

The two contributions of this paper — the Markov memory ladder and the survey-design diagnostic toolkit — are portable across datasets and topological methods. The ladder should be climbed with both scalar and diagram-level statistics; omitting either risks false conclusions. The survey-design diagnostics should be applied whenever linked panel data spans survey instruments with different sampling frames. These are not specialist tools for topological data analysis; they are general-purpose methodological aids for any researcher who wants to know whether the structure they find in a longitudinal point cloud is real.
